The Frustration

ChatGPT Gave Me A Generic Answer

Ask ChatGPT a question cold and you'll get the average of every internet answer it has ever read about that topic. Generic structure. Generic phrasing. Generic caveats. The output is technically correct and practically useless — the kind of answer you could have written yourself if you'd had ten more minutes and a coffee.

The problem isn't that the model doesn't know enough. It's that without a role, it has no reason to commit to a perspective. It defaults to safe, average, hedged. The first frame of this video is a flat, bland response on the left, and the polished expert-level version on the right. The only thing that changed between them was a single sentence added before the question.

Three things from the prior batch shaped the choice of role-based prompting as the #2 video specifically:

  • Named tools win every algorithm. The breakout AI-tools video earned 1,011 views with the title "Google Drive AI: Read All Your Files Instantly." It led with a tool everyone uses and an instant, concrete benefit. ChatGPT in every title of this Vol follows that pattern.
  • Insider jargon kills CTR. Titles like "Midjourney V8.1" and "OpenAI Prism" flopped — viewers don't recognize those terms in 0.5 seconds of swiping.   • The "Stop using X" pattern is burned. Five videos in a row used it in the previous batch and the last two got 0% CTR on 100+ impressions. V11 intentionally uses an "I-did-this + outcome" pattern instead — the "proven format" the brief reserved for the channel's #2-impression slot.
The Fix

Give ChatGPT A Job Title

The full prompt pattern is one sentence longer than what you'd usually type, and it changes everything about how the model approaches the answer:

// The Role-Based Prompting Template
Act as a [job title] with [X] years of experience in [domain]. Then answer: [your question here]

The three variables matter for three different reasons:

  • Job title — picks the vocabulary and the priorities the model uses to organize the answer. "Senior marketing manager" reaches for funnels, conversion, brand voice. "Cardiologist" reaches for anatomy, risk factors, differential diagnosis.
  • Years of experience — implicitly sets the depth. "5 years" produces a tactical, hands-on answer. "20 years" produces a strategic, pattern-recognition answer. Both are useful; pick which one you actually need.
  • Domain — narrows the scope. "Cardiologist who specializes in cardiac rehab" filters out generic cardiology and zeroes in on recovery protocols and lifestyle intervention. Without the domain, the model spans the field.

The video shows the same base question — "What should I focus on first?" — asked three times with three different personas. Doctor, financial advisor, UX designer. Three completely different answers, each one usable for the audience that persona is built for.

"The secret: give AI a job title."

— On-screen text, V11, 6.2s
Why It Works

Personas Are Conditioning Vectors

Under the hood, a persona prompt isn't a magic spell — it's an additional conditioning vector. Every token in your prompt shifts the probability distribution the model samples from. Adding "Act as a senior marketing manager" doesn't change the model's weights, but it shifts where in latent space it draws the next-token probabilities from. The model is now more likely to choose marketing-manager phrasing, marketing-manager structure, marketing-manager priorities.

The most-cited effect size in the prompt-engineering community for role conditioning is a 30–40% lift in subjective answer quality, depending on the task. The on-screen overlay in the video uses 40% as the upper bound. That number isn't from a single paper — it's the rough convergence of small experiments people have run on writing-style transfer, technical advice, and structured analysis tasks. Take it as an order-of-magnitude signal, not a precise measurement.

What matters in practice: the same model, same temperature, same question — but the answer becomes recognizably coming from somewhere. It has a vocabulary. It has priorities. It has a structure. That's worth one sentence at the start of every prompt that needs to feel grounded.

The Catch

Persona Doesn't Make It True

Role-based prompting changes how the model answers — not whether the answer is correct. A "senior cardiologist" persona doesn't give the model new clinical knowledge. It gives it a voice. If the underlying knowledge is wrong, the persona will deliver wrong information with more confidence and more clinical-sounding vocabulary. That's a worse failure mode than a generic answer that hedges its way out of trouble.

The fix for the truth problem is in later videos in this Vol — delimiters (V15), negative constraints (V17), and RAG (V19). Role-based prompting is the first move, not the last. Stack it with the others when stakes are high.

Second catch: too generic a persona dilutes the effect. "Act as an expert" is barely better than no persona at all — the model can't infer specific vocabulary or priorities from "expert" alone. The sentence needs a real job title and ideally a years-of-experience number to do work.

Real Use Cases

The Five Personas That Earn Their Sentence

  • Senior marketing manager, 15 years — turns a generic "write a product description" into one with hook, body, social-proof line, and call-to-action.
  • Cardiologist, 20 years — turns "is this chest pain serious?" into a triaged differential with red-flag criteria and when-to-call-911 language. (Still not medical advice. Still call your doctor.)
  • Financial advisor, 10 years — turns "should I pay off my car loan or invest?" into an interest-rate-vs-expected-return calculation with risk tolerance considered.
  • Senior UX designer, 12 years — turns "what's wrong with this checkout flow?" into a structured heuristic walkthrough citing Nielsen rules, conversion-blockers, and accessibility gaps.
  • Investigative journalist, 15 years — turns "how do I find sources for this story?" into a step-by-step framework for FOIA requests, court records, and adversarial interview prep.

Common Mistake

Don't Use "Expert" By Itself

The single most common failure mode is the vague persona. "Act as an expert in this area" or "you are a knowledgeable professional" — neither of these gives the model a real conditioning signal. "Expert" doesn't have its own vocabulary or priorities. The output will be marginally better than no persona at all, and you'll wonder why this technique is supposed to be such a big deal.

The fix is concreteness. A real job title, a real number of years, a real domain. "Cardiologist with 20 years of experience specializing in cardiac rehab" beats "experienced doctor" by an embarrassing margin — same model, same temperature, same question. Try them back-to-back on whatever you're working on right now and the difference is the entire point of this video.

// Takeaway

One sentence at the start of every prompt that needs depth. Real job title, real years, real domain. The model goes from "average of the internet" to "voice of someone specific" — and that's worth more than another five sentences of clarification.

Don't stack five techniques on V11. Just start every prompt with the role. Layer on the rest as you learn them through this series.
