The Frustration

AI Just Made Up A Fact. Confidently.

You ask ChatGPT for a statistic, a citation, a date — and it gives you one, stated with total confidence. Then you check, and it's wrong. Not "roughly right" wrong; invented-out-of-thin-air wrong, complete with a fake source. This is a hallucination, and it's the single most dangerous failure mode in AI, because the wrong answer looks exactly as authoritative as a right one. IBM's plain definition: "a hallucination or confabulation happens when models present incorrect or made-up information as though it is factual."

The reason it happens is structural. A language model answers from patterns in its training data — and when it doesn't actually know something, it fills the gap with the most plausible-sounding guess rather than admitting ignorance. AWS has the perfect analogy: an ungrounded model is like "an over-enthusiastic new employee who refuses to stay informed with current events but will always answer every question with absolute confidence." The fix isn't to hope for a smarter model. It's to stop letting the model answer from memory and force it to answer from a source you give it. Paste your document, tell it to use only that, and the made-up facts vanish — because the model is no longer guessing, it's quoting.

The Fix

Paste Your Source. Say "Use Only This."

The entire technique is two moves: give the model your source, and tell it to answer only from that source. This is the consumer-grade version of RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation — which AWS defines as "the process of optimizing the output of a large language model, so it references an authoritative knowledge base outside of its training data sources before generating a response." Enterprise RAG automates the "retrieve" step with vector databases; you do it by hand with copy-paste. Same principle, same result:

// The "Poor Man's RAG" Prompt
Answer the question using ONLY the text below. If the answer isn't in the text, say "I don't know." === SOURCE === [paste your article, doc, contract, or notes here] === END SOURCE === Question: [your question]

The load-bearing line is "if the answer isn't in the text, say 'I don't know.'" Without it, the model falls back to guessing the moment your source comes up short — and you're back to hallucinations. With it, you give the model explicit permission to admit ignorance, which is the one thing it never does on its own. The grounding flips where the answer comes from. AWS frames the contrast exactly: without RAG, "the LLM... creates a response based on information it was trained on — or what it already knows"; with RAG, "the user query and the relevant information are both given to the LLM." Your pasted source is the relevant information.

GeeksforGeeks sums up the payoff in one line: RAG "reduces hallucinations by retrieving verified, external data to ground responses in factual knowledge." You're not making the model smarter — you're changing what it's allowed to draw from. And the bonus, per IBM: a grounded answer can cite its source, so "human users can verify those outputs to confirm accuracy." No more taking a confident answer on faith.

"Paste your source into the prompt. Then say, answer only from this text. If you don't know, say so. Done."

— V19 VO, 0:04 – 0:10
Why It Works

It Quotes Instead Of Guessing

A normal prompt asks the model to answer from everything it absorbed during training — a vast, fuzzy, occasionally-wrong memory with no way to tell you which parts it's sure about. When that memory comes up empty, the model doesn't stop; it generates the most statistically plausible continuation, which is exactly how a confident wrong answer is born. IBM's description is precise: a model "work[s] by detecting patterns in their data, then using those patterns to predict the most likely outcomes to user inputs. Sometimes models detect patterns that don't exist."

Pasting a source changes the question the model is answering. Instead of "what's the most likely answer from everything I know," it becomes "what does this specific text say." AWS draws the line cleanly: without grounding, "the LLM... creates a response based on information it was trained on — or what it already knows"; with grounding, "the user query and the relevant information are both given to the LLM." The relevant information — your pasted document — is now the authority, and the model's job shrinks from recall to reading comprehension, a task it's far more reliable at.

GeeksforGeeks states the result directly: RAG "reduces hallucinations by retrieving verified, external data to ground responses in factual knowledge" and "fetches up-to-date data and reduces incorrect or made-up answers." Two benefits fall out of that grounding for free:

  • Citations you can verify — because the answer comes from a text you can see, you can check it. IBM: a grounded model can "include citations to the knowledge sources... human users can verify those outputs to confirm accuracy." The answer stops being an act of faith.
  • Current and private knowledge — your source can be anything: today's report, a contract the model never trained on, your own notes. RAG, per IBM, "anchors LLMs in specific knowledge backed by factual, authoritative and current data," sidestepping both the training cutoff and the limits of public data.

"RAG anchors LLMs in specific knowledge backed by factual, authoritative and current data... While RAG can reduce the risk of hallucinations, it cannot make a model error-proof."

— IBM · Retrieval-Augmented Generation · 2026
The Catch

Grounded Isn't The Same As Error-Proof

The honest caveat is the one IBM puts right in its own definition: "while RAG can reduce the risk of hallucinations, it cannot make a model error-proof." Grounding shifts where the answer comes from, but the model can still misread your source, stitch two passages together wrong, or quietly fall back to its training if your instruction is weak. Pasting a source dramatically lowers the hallucination rate; it doesn't zero it. Verify anything that matters — the technique makes verification possible (the source is right there), it doesn't make it unnecessary.

Second catch: the answer is only as good as the source you paste. RAG's reliability, GeeksforGeeks notes, has a "heavy dependence on retrieval quality" and a "risk of inheriting biases from source documents." Paste a source that's outdated, partial, or wrong, and you'll get a confident answer that's faithfully grounded in bad information. Garbage in, grounded garbage out. The model can't fact-check the document you handed it — it can only stay inside it.

Third catch: you must actually constrain it. The grounding only holds if you tell the model to stay inside the source and give it an exit when the source falls short. Drop the line "if the answer isn't in the text, say 'I don't know,'" and the model treats your source as a starting point rather than a boundary — answering partly from the text and partly from memory, which is the worst of both worlds because you can no longer tell which is which. The constraint is the technique; the source is just the input.

And the meta-catch this video demonstrated: "paste a source" is something most people half-already-know. The save data (2 on TikTok, 0 on Instagram) suggests the tip wasn't novel enough to capture — unlike the surprise of "start every prompt with a verb" or "tell it what NOT to do," grounding-by-paste feels like common sense once stated. A tactic only earns a save when it teaches something the viewer didn't already suspect. The data section below has the full picture.

Real Use Cases

Where Grounding Is Non-Negotiable

Anywhere a wrong answer has a cost, paste the source. Five cases where grounding turns ChatGPT from a confident guesser into a reliable reader — the domains IBM, AWS, and GeeksforGeeks all flag:

  • Legal and contract review — paste the contract, ask "what does clause 7 say about termination, using only this document." A hallucinated contract term is a liability; a grounded one is a quote. GeeksforGeeks specifically calls out "specialized domains like medical or legal content" as where RAG matters most.
  • Research summaries — paste the paper, ask for the findings "from the text only." This is the entire reason the technique exists: stop the model from inventing a statistic that sounds right. The exact failure V19's video opens on.
  • Customer support from your own docs — paste the FAQ or policy, answer from it. AWS's canonical RAG use case: an HR bot answering "how much annual leave do I have?" from the actual policy doc, not a plausible-sounding guess.
  • Product specs and technical facts — paste the spec sheet before asking about dimensions, compatibility, or limits. The model's training has a vague, possibly-outdated memory of your product; your spec sheet is the truth.
  • Anything past the training cutoff — today's news, this quarter's numbers, a document written last week. IBM: RAG "connect[s] models with supplemental external data in real-time and incorporate[s] up-to-date information into generated responses," covering exactly what the model couldn't have learned.

The through-line: the moment a wrong answer would cost you — money, credibility, a legal exposure — don't let the model answer from memory. Paste the source, constrain it to that source, and give it permission to say "I don't know." You've turned a confident guesser into a careful reader.

Common Mistake

Pasting The Source But Not Constraining It

The most common mis-application is the one that quietly undoes the whole technique: you paste your source but forget to tell the model to stay inside it. "Here's the document, now answer my question" is not RAG — it's a hint. The model treats your text as additional context layered on top of its training, not as the only permitted source, so it freely mixes pasted facts with remembered ones. You end up with an answer that's part grounded and part hallucinated, which is worse than either, because you can no longer tell which sentences came from your document. The fix is two non-negotiable lines: "answer only from the text below" and "if the answer isn't there, say you don't know."

The second mistake is trusting a grounded answer without verifying it. Grounding lowers the hallucination rate dramatically — it doesn't zero it. IBM is explicit: RAG "cannot make a model error-proof." The model can still misread your source or stitch two passages together wrong. The technique's real gift is that it makes verification cheap: the source is right there, so you can check the answer against it in seconds. Use that gift on anything that matters; don't let "grounded" lull you into "guaranteed."

The third mistake is pasting a bad source. RAG inherits the quality of what you feed it — GeeksforGeeks flags the "risk of inheriting biases from source documents" and a "heavy dependence on retrieval quality." A confident answer grounded in an outdated spec sheet or a one-sided article is still wrong; it's just wrong faithfully. Grounding controls hallucination, not accuracy. The source has to be right for the answer to be right.

// Takeaway

When a wrong answer would cost you, stop letting ChatGPT answer from memory. Paste your source, then add the two lines that do the work: answer only from this text and if it's not there, say you don't know. The model stops guessing and starts quoting — and the made-up facts disappear, because you've changed its job from recall to reading comprehension. That's RAG, done by hand.

Two guardrails keep it honest: the constraint line is the technique (a pasted source without it is just a hint), and grounded is not error-proof (the source has to be right, and you should still verify what matters). Your AI, your data, your rules — but only if you set the rules explicitly.

// Ryu's Read On The Data

V19 is the experiment I didn't mean to run, and it settled the whole question. I fixed everything the last losses blamed: a tactic, not a workflow. Perfect search targeting — the query was literally "chat gpt hallucinating," the exact problem. An early queue, #3 on TikTok. The most relatable topic in the Vol. And it still died at 0:02, with the lowest average watch time I've ever posted (3.69s). When you control for everything and lose anyway, the answer is obvious: it's the hook.

The hook was "AI just made up a fact. Here's how to stop it" — the most generic opening format there is. Problem statement, "here's the fix." The scroll-brain has seen it ten thousand times and swipes on reflex. V17 won by breaking that template — naming what you felt, in second person. So the rule for V20 is locked: never open with "X problem, here's the fix." Open with the feeling, in the first two seconds, or nothing else matters.

And a quieter lesson: being a tactic isn't enough to get saved. "Paste your source" is one move, like verbs and do-nots — but it earned 2 saves and 1 like total. The difference is novelty. Verbs felt like a secret; pasting a source feels obvious once said. A save is a bet on future need, and nobody bets on what they think they already know. Niche held a 5th time (95% over-35), followers at zero a 9th. The follow fix is still the one thing I haven't rebuilt — and it's next.
