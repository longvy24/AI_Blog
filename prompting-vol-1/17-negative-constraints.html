The Frustration

You Got What You Asked For. Not What You Wanted.

You ask ChatGPT a simple question and get back a 600-word wall: a preamble that restates your question, three paragraphs of throat-clearing, a bulleted list with eleven bullets, jargon you'd never use, and a closing summary that repeats everything you just read. Technically it answered you. Practically you now have to edit the answer — which is the work you were trying to skip.

The reason is structural: an AI fills whatever space it's given. Ask it to "explain X" and it will produce the most expansive, hedge-everything, cover-all-bases version, because that's the safe center of its training distribution. Telling it to be "concise" or "clear" barely helps — those are vibes, not boundaries. The fix is to flip the instruction from positive to negative: instead of describing the good output you want, name the bad outputs you don't. "Do not use jargon. Do not include a preamble. Do not exceed five bullets." Each "do not" is a wall, and the walls together leave only the clean answer standing.

The Fix

Three "Do Nots." Then Four To Steal.

A negative constraint is an instruction phrased as a prohibition: not "be concise," but "do not exceed five bullets." The difference matters because a prohibition is checkable — the model can verify whether it broke the rule, where "be concise" has no bright line. Start with three that fix the most common AI-fluff failure modes:

// The 3 Core "Do Nots" · Add To Any Prompt
[your normal prompt] Constraints: - Do not use jargon. - Do not include a preamble. Start with the answer. - Do not exceed 5 bullet points. → Output goes from a 600-word wall to a clean, scannable answer — same model, same question.

Each of those targets a specific AI habit: "do not use jargon" cuts the vocabulary the model defaults to when it's trying to sound authoritative; "do not include a preamble" kills the "Great question! Let's explore..." throat-clearing; "do not exceed 5 bullets" caps the model's tendency to pad a list until it looks thorough. Once you've seen the pattern, the toolkit extends. Four more worth stealing:

// 4 More To Steal
- Do not use passive voice. - Do not repeat yourself or restate the question. - Do not exceed 100 words. - Do not give generic advice — be specific to my case.

The mechanism behind why this works so well is more interesting than the tactic itself, and it has academic backing. A 2026 position paper from Tsinghua — "Via Negativa for AI Alignment" — argues that negative constraints are structurally superior to positive instructions, because "negative constraints encode discrete, finite, independently verifiable prohibitions that can converge to a stable boundary," where positive preferences are "continuously coupled, context-dependent" and can't be fully specified. In plain terms: "do not X" is a clean wall the model can check itself against; "be good" is a direction with no edge.

"Constraints equal control. AI fills space. You decide how. Add one 'do not' to your next prompt and watch it transform."

Why It Works

A "Do Not" Is A Wall That Stays Put

Here's the structural reason negative constraints beat positive ones, and it's the most interesting idea in this whole video. A positive instruction ("be concise," "write clearly," "be helpful") points the model in a direction, but the direction has no edge — the model can always argue it was being concise. A negative constraint ("do not exceed 100 words") is a wall the model can check itself against. One is a vibe; the other is a verifiable line.

The Tsinghua position paper "Via Negativa for AI Alignment" (Quan Cheng, 2026) makes this asymmetry its central thesis: "positive preferences encode continuously coupled, context-dependent human values that cannot be exhaustively specified... negative constraints encode discrete, finite, independently verifiable prohibitions that can converge to a stable boundary." The practical payoff is what happens when you stack them:

"As the number of negative constraints increases, the feasible response space contracts monotonically. Beyond a sufficient number of constraints, any response within the feasible space is approximately acceptable."

— Quan Cheng · Via Negativa for AI Alignment · arXiv 2026

That sentence is the entire technique in one line. Every "do not" you add shrinks the space of allowed outputs — and crucially, the constraints are cumulative and non-conflicting: in Cheng's words, "adding a new prohibition never re-opens a previously closed region." Positive instructions fight each other ("be detailed" vs. "be brief"); prohibitions stack cleanly. Three "do nots" don't compete — they each carve off a slice of bad output, and what's left is the answer you wanted. The paper even offers a testable prediction that matches what every prompt engineer notices in practice: "more capable models possess more negative knowledge (what not to say) rather than more positive knowledge (what to say). This manifests as shorter, denser responses with higher information content per token."

The grounding is older than LLMs. Cheng traces the asymmetry to Karl Popper's falsification logic — "a single counterexample decisively refutes a universal claim, but no finite number of confirming instances can decisively verify one" — and to Nassim Taleb's via negativa: "in domains of high uncertainty, removing what is harmful is more robust than adding what seems beneficial." Telling ChatGPT what not to do is the prompt-engineering version of that principle.

The Catch

Don't Think Of A Pink Elephant

There's a real failure mode, and it's the opposite of what the academic case predicts. Negative constraints work beautifully when they prohibit a category ("no jargon," "no preamble"). They can backfire when they name a specific word or thing — because naming it puts it in front of the model. Smart Prompts for AI frames the mechanism exactly: "LLMs are prediction engines... when you say, 'Do not use the word delve,' you have just injected the token 'delve' into the context window." It's the don't-think-of-a-pink-elephant problem. To process the prohibition, the model has to hold the forbidden thing in mind — and sometimes that priming wins.

So the rule that reconciles the two sources is this: constrain behaviors and categories, not specific tokens. "Do not use jargon" works because "jargon" is a category the model suppresses. "Do not use the word 'delve'" can misfire because you've just made "delve" salient. When you genuinely need to ban a specific word, the safer phrasing is positive — "prefer plain synonyms" — or you give the replacement directly: "use 'look into' instead of 'delve.'"

Second catch — and this one comes from the academic side, in fairness: prohibitions can't specify everything. Cheng's paper is careful to bound its own claim: "we do not claim that negative constraints are sufficient for all aspects of alignment. Certain alignment desiderata — helpfulness, creativity, appropriate tone — are inherently positive." The same is true at the prompt level. "Do not" trims fluff and enforces shape, but it can't create a voice, an angle, or an insight. Negative constraints are a sculptor's chisel, not a brush — they remove, they don't add. Pair them with the positive instructions (role, format, the verb from V16) that supply the substance.

Third catch: over-constraining strangles the output. The "feasible space contracts monotonically" property cuts both ways. Stack fifteen "do nots" and you can squeeze the space so tight the model produces something stilted, hedged, or empty — technically legal under every rule and useful under none. Three to five prohibitions is the sweet spot for an everyday prompt; past that you're not sculpting, you're suffocating.

Real Use Cases

Where "Do Not" Earns Its Keystrokes

Five everyday situations where a negative constraint fixes the output faster than any positive instruction could. The pattern is always the same — name the specific bad habit you keep getting, and prohibit it:

  • Killing AI fluff in any draft — the canonical case. "Do not include a preamble. Do not restate my question. Do not add a summary at the end." These three remove the throat-clearing that makes AI writing instantly recognizable, and they work on emails, posts, and reports alike.
  • Forcing concision — "Do not exceed 100 words" beats "be brief" every time, because it's a wall, not a wish. The model overshoots a vague "keep it short" by 30-40%; a hard word cap it respects. Pair with "do not use filler phrases like 'it's important to note.'"
  • Getting specific advice instead of platitudes — "Do not give generic advice. Every point must reference my specific situation." This single prohibition is the difference between "communicate clearly and set boundaries" and actual tailored guidance. It forces the model out of the safe, universal-truth register.
  • Cleaning up tone — "Do not use passive voice. Do not hedge with 'might,' 'could,' or 'perhaps.'" Prohibiting the hedge words directly produces confident, declarative prose — useful for anything that needs to sound decisive rather than diplomatic.
  • Controlling format — "Do not exceed 5 bullets. Do not nest sub-bullets. Do not bold every other word." The model pads lists and over-formats by default; capping the structure keeps the output scannable instead of a Christmas tree of bold and indentation.

The through-line: a negative constraint is most powerful when you've already seen the bad habit. After a few prompts you learn the model's tells — the preamble, the eleven-bullet list, the "in today's fast-paced world." Each tell becomes a "do not," and your prompts get a permanent personal blocklist that makes every future output cleaner from the first try.

Common Mistake

A Vague "Do Not" Is Just A Vibe

The most common mis-application defeats the entire point of the technique: writing a negative constraint that isn't checkable. "Do not be boring," "do not be generic," "do not make it bad" — these are prohibitions in grammar only. They have no bright line, so the model can't verify whether it broke them, which is the exact failure mode of vague positive instructions like "be engaging." The whole reason "do not" beats "do" is that a good prohibition is a wall the model can measure itself against. A vague one isn't a wall; it's a mood.

The fix is to make every "do not" something a machine could check. Not "do not be wordy" but "do not exceed 100 words." Not "do not be generic" but "do not give advice that would apply to anyone — every point must reference my specific situation." Not "do not be robotic" but "do not use the phrases 'it's important to note' or 'in conclusion.'" Each rewrite swaps a feeling for a testable condition, and the testable version is the one the model can actually obey.

The second mistake is forgetting the lesson from §5: constrain categories, not specific tokens. "Do not use jargon" suppresses a category and works; "do not say the word 'leverage'" can prime the very word you banned. When you must ban a specific term, give the replacement instead of the prohibition — "use 'use' instead of 'leverage'" — so you're pointing the model at the target rather than at the thing to avoid.

// Takeaway

Add three "do nots" to your next prompt: no jargon, no preamble, max 5 bullets. The output goes from a 600-word wall to a clean, scannable answer — same model, same question. A prohibition is a checkable wall where "be concise" is just a wish, and walls stack cleanly: every "do not" carves off bad output without re-opening what the last one closed.

Two rules keep it working: make each "do not" checkable (a word count, not a vibe), and constrain categories, not specific tokens (naming a banned word can summon it). Negative constraints are a chisel — they remove, they don't add — so pair them with the role, format, and verb that supply the substance. AI fills whatever space you leave. Decide how.
