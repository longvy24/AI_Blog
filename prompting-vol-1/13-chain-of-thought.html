The Frustration

AI Got The Math Wrong

You ask ChatGPT to solve a word problem. It gives you a confident, wrong answer. You ask it again, more clearly. It gives you a different confident, wrong answer. You verify by hand — the model is making errors in steps a fifth-grader wouldn't miss. And the prose around the wrong answer reads like the model is certain, which makes it worse: you almost shipped that number to a spreadsheet.

This isn't a "the model is bad at math" problem. It's a "the model is being asked to answer instead of reason" problem. Modern LLMs can do this kind of arithmetic reliably — but only if you change one thing about the prompt. Three words, specifically.

The Fix

Add "Think Step By Step"

The full template fits on one line, appended to any prompt where you want the model to reason instead of guess:

// The Chain-Of-Thought Template
[your question here] Let's think step by step.

Three variants do the same job — pick whichever phrasing feels natural to you. IBM's reference on the technique cites all three:

  • "Let's think step by step." — the canonical phrasing from Kojima et al. (2022). DataCamp's tutorial puts it bluntly: this phrase, "proposed by University of Tokyo and Google Research researchers," is the implicit instruction that prompts models to reason aloud.
  • "Describe your reasoning steps." — IBM's preferred phrasing for explicit decomposition. Same effect; reads more formal.
  • "Explain your answer step-by-step." — what IBM calls the most common appended instruction. The model is asked not just to generate a result but also detail the series of intermediate steps that led to that answer.

The lift these three words produce is documented and dramatic. The headline result is from the Wei & Kojima et al. work on the MultiArith math dataset:

"Applying this simple trick to the MultiArith math dataset, the authors found that this implicit instruction quadrupled the accuracy from 18% to 79%."

— DataCamp · Andrea Valenzuela, July 2024

Quadrupled. Same model. Same dataset. Three additional words. V13's claim that this "cuts AI errors in half" is, if anything, conservative — on the published benchmark, the lift was larger than that.

Why It Works

Decomposition Beats Guessing

The mechanism is straightforward. Without "think step by step," the model is essentially being asked to jump to a final answer — generating the response token-by-token without first producing the intermediate work. For arithmetic, scheduling, or any task that requires holding several constraints in memory at once, that's structurally the wrong shape of computation. DataCamp's tutorial frames it precisely:

"The decomposition of the reasoning process makes the model focus its attention on one part of the problem at a time, minimizing the risk of errors that might arise from handling too much information simultaneously."

— DataCamp · Andrea Valenzuela, July 2024

IBM frames the same idea historically. The original 2022 Wei et al. paper proposed that "prompting models to generate intermediate reasoning steps significantly boosts their ability to accurately solve multistep problems like arithmetic, common sense and symbolic reasoning." The technique simulates human-like reasoning — "breaking down elaborate problems into manageable, intermediate steps that sequentially lead to a conclusive answer."

The promptingguide.ai community reference on CoT — citing Wei et al. (2022) — adds one more practical observation: chain-of-thought composes with few-shot prompting. You can "combine it with few-shot prompting to get better results on more complex tasks that require reasoning before responding." Showing three worked examples with reasoning steps in each is the most powerful form of the technique. V12's few-shot template + V13's CoT trigger phrase together are stronger than either alone.

The Catch

Don't Use It On Simple Asks

Chain-of-thought is not a free upgrade. DataCamp's tutorial flags the first failure mode directly: "CoT prompting may not be as effective for tasks that do not have a clear sequential reasoning process." Asking a model to "think step by step" before a one-word answer (sentiment classification, language detection, simple lookup) doesn't help — it just adds latency and tokens to a response that was always going to be one word.

Second catch: computational cost. IBM lists this directly among CoT's documented limitations: "the need for high-quality prompts, increased computational cost, susceptibility to adversarial attacks and challenges in evaluating quality." A reasoning trace can easily 3-5x the output tokens versus a direct answer. On free-tier models that's a context-window concern; on metered API usage it's a real dollar cost.

Third catch: plausible but wrong reasoning. A confident-looking reasoning trace can still arrive at a wrong answer. The chain-of-thought makes errors more traceable, not less likely to happen — what improves is debuggability, not infallibility. When the model's intermediate step 3 is wrong, you can spot it and re-prompt. Without CoT, you just see the final wrong number with no way to find the failure.

Real Use Cases

Five Places Where Reasoning Helps

The pattern recurs across every domain that requires holding constraints, working through implications, or composing partial answers. IBM's reference catalogs CoT use in educational technology, scientific reasoning, and step-by-step problem-solving. Five concrete applications worth adding to your prompt library:

  • Multi-step math and arithmetic. The original Wei et al. result was a math benchmark — and that's still the cleanest demonstration. Anywhere you need calculation rather than recall, append "Let's think step by step."
  • Scheduling and logistics. V13's first demo: "Scheduling problem, it maps the constraints first then solves." Anything involving time slots, dependencies, or resource constraints benefits from the model writing out the constraint set before attempting a solution.
  • Business decisions with trade-offs. V13's second demo: "Business decision, it weighs the trade-offs out loud then concludes." Pricing, vendor selection, build-vs-buy — anywhere the right answer depends on weighing multiple factors, the explicit weighting beats the snap judgment.
  • Educational explanation. IBM specifically highlights CoT in "educational technology platforms, aiding in the generation of step-by-step explanations for complex problems" in math and science. If you're producing teaching material, CoT-prompted output is structurally what students need to read.
  • Code debugging and review. Promptingguide.ai documents combining few-shot CoT with code tasks. Ask the model to walk through the function's behavior step-by-step before identifying the bug, and the diagnosis is dramatically more accurate than asking "what's wrong with this code?" cold.

Five findings, including one that validates the brief's per-platform queueing strategy:

(1) Strategic #1-slot worked on YouTube. Brief-prediction validated. The publishing strategy positioned V13 first on YouTube specifically because the title-hook combination was rated as "the strongest in the set." Whatever criteria the brief used to rank YT hooks is repeatable and worth applying to V14-V20's title selection.

(2) Strategic #1-slot was mixed on TikTok. Partial. The brief predicted V13 would have the strongest engagement signal because of the comment-bait setup. V13 did pick up the comment (1, same as V11) — but lost on saves (1.42% rate vs V11's 2.1% and V12's 2.7%) and on raw views (141 vs ~188). TikTok's algorithm doesn't reward what the brief defined as "shareability" the way the brief expected. For V14+, the brief's TikTok ranking should be re-validated against the actual engagement curve V13/V11/V12 produced.

(3) Shorter runtime lifts completion. Confirmed. V13 at 40s broke the 0.5% completion ceiling — 0.7% full-watch rate, a 40% relative improvement over V11/V12's 45s. The runtime lever is real. Every V14+ video should target 35-40s, not 45s.

(4) Quantified CTAs don't automatically outperform. Disconfirmed. V13's "cuts AI errors in half" saved at 1.42%, below V11's 2.1% and V12's 2.7%. The CTA isn't about whether there's a number in it — it's about whether the number connects to something the viewer was already trying to do. V12's "consistent AI" tied directly to the format-control pain point. V13's "cuts errors in half" was impressive but disconnected from the specific scenarios shown.

(5) Feed-CTR vs off-feed reach are different titling games. New finding. V13's "ChatGPT Is Lying to You" earned feed CTR (0.35%) and 5× the impressions. V12's "How to Make ChatGPT Copy Your Writing Style" — with 0% CTR — won 3× more total views from off-feed surfaces (Shorts swipes, search, recommendations). Provocative titles win feed slots; descriptive titles win search and discovery. The brief positioned V13 for feed dominance and V12 for niche audiences — both worked on their respective surfaces, which is why the queue strategy held despite the surface-level appearance that V12 "did better" than V13 on YouTube views.

The unsolved problem stays unsolved. 0 followers across 850 cumulative views and three videos. The brief's queue strategy is winning where it predicted (YouTube CTR), losing where it predicted differently (TikTok engagement), and silent on the follower-conversion problem that no CTA tweak has moved. V14 needs a structural change to the last 2 seconds — probably a channel-branded moment, not another CTA variant.

Common Mistake

Don't Append It To Everything

The most common over-application is using "think step by step" on prompts that don't need it. If you're asking for a sentiment classification, a language detection, a yes/no answer, or a one-word lookup — adding the CoT trigger phrase doesn't help. DataCamp's exact warning: "CoT prompting may not be as effective for tasks that do not have a clear sequential reasoning process." You'll just burn tokens and add latency to a question that was always going to be one token.

The other failure mode is trusting the trace at face value. A confident-looking reasoning chain can still arrive at the wrong answer — what you've gained is visibility into where the model went wrong, which lets you re-prompt. CoT makes mistakes auditable, not impossible.

// Takeaway

Three words at the end of every prompt that involves math, scheduling, multi-step logic, or trade-off analysis. Let's think step by step. The model writes its reasoning and the accuracy lift is documented — quadrupled on the original MultiArith benchmark per Wei & Kojima et al.

Combine with role-based prompting (V11) for tone and few-shot prompting (V12) for structure — three techniques layered into one prompt is dramatically more reliable than any of them alone.
