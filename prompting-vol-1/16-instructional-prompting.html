The Frustration

"Help Me With My Email" Gets You Trash

You type "help me with my email" and ChatGPT hands back something generic — a vague rewrite, a question asking what you want, or a wall of options you didn't ask for. You rephrase. Still mushy. The instinct is to blame the model, but the model did exactly what you told it to: nothing specific, because you gave it nothing specific to do. "Help" isn't a task. It's a vibe.

The fix is the smallest verbal change in the entire toolkit, and it's the one that changes the most: start the prompt with a command verb. Not "help me with my email" — "Write a three-sentence follow-up email with a professional tone and a clear call-to-action." Same intent, completely different result. Large language models are instruction-followers by design. Give them an instruction and they execute; give them a hint and they guess. This is instructional prompting, and it's the most universal habit in prompt engineering because it costs one word and works on literally every prompt you'll ever write.

The Fix

Five Verbs. Start Here.

An instruction prompt starts with a verb that names the task. GeeksforGeeks defines the technique as "providing clear, specific and structured natural language instructions to a generative AI model... to guide them in performing complex tasks accurately and efficiently" — and the simplest way to do that is to lead with the command. These five verbs cover the overwhelming majority of everyday prompts, and each one steers the model toward a completely different output shape:

// The 5 Starter Verbs · Same Topic, Different Verb, Different Output
Topic: a research paper you pasted in. SUMMARIZE → condense it "Summarize this paper in 100 words." → tight overview, key findings only. WRITE → produce something new from it "Write a LinkedIn post based on this paper's conclusion." → original content in your voice. ANALYZE → break it down / find structure "Analyze the methodology and flag any weaknesses." → critical evaluation, not a recap. COMPARE → set two things against each other "Compare this paper's findings to the prior consensus." → side-by-side, differences surfaced. LIST → enumerate into a scannable format "List the five main takeaways as bullet points." → structured, save-able, skimmable.

The point of the demo isn't the five verbs specifically — it's that the verb is the task. Same pasted paper, five different commands, five genuinely different and useful outputs. Learn Prompting frames the underlying capability precisely: instruction prompting "aims to allow models to work with new, previously unseen tasks by understanding and executing instructions expressed in natural language, without requiring task-specific training data." You don't show the model examples of what you want. You tell it.

The most common upgrade is to stack a verb with constraints — GeeksforGeeks' canonical examples all do this: "Summarize the article in 100 words," "List five pros and cons of electric vehicles in a table format," "Write a 500-word blog post explaining how AI is transforming healthcare, focusing on diagnostics and patient care." The verb sets the task; the constraints (length, format, focus) sharpen it. But the verb comes first, and a bare verb already beats a vague hint every time.

"Verb equals task. Be direct. AI responds to commands. Start every prompt with a verb. That's the whole hack."

— V16 VO, 0:17 – 0:29
Why It Works

The Model Was Built To Obey Verbs

Modern LLMs aren't just text predictors — they're instruction-tuned. After pretraining, they're fine-tuned on millions of (instruction → ideal response) pairs, which is precisely why they respond so sharply to a clear command and so vaguely to a hint. When you start with a verb, you're speaking the exact dialect the model was optimized to follow. GeeksforGeeks puts the capability plainly: instruction prompting "leverages the model's pretrained knowledge and language understanding to generalize to new, unseen tasks based solely on the instructions provided." No examples required. Just the command.

GeeksforGeeks catalogs five concrete reasons the technique carries so much weight:

  • Flexibility & scalability — "Instruction prompting allows AI to adapt quickly to new tasks without retraining or large labeled datasets, making it highly scalable across domains and applications."
  • Precision & control — "Well-crafted instructions help reduce ambiguity, leading to more accurate and relevant outputs." This is the whole game: a verb removes the ambiguity that "help me" leaves wide open.
  • Accessibility — "Users without technical or programming expertise can interact effectively with AI by simply providing clear instructions in natural language." No syntax to learn — just say what you want done.
  • Cost efficiency — "Reduces the need for expensive custom model training or data labeling by focusing on prompt design rather than model modification."
  • Iterative improvement — "Instructions can be refined iteratively to improve output quality and mitigate errors." Start with a verb, then sharpen it with constraints — exactly the workflow V14 documented.

The reason a verb beats a hint is mechanical, not stylistic. "Help me with my email" forces the model to first infer the task (rewrite? draft? reply? proofread?) and then perform it — and it usually infers conservatively, handing back something safe and generic. "Write a three-sentence reply declining the meeting" skips the inference step entirely. The task is named; the model goes straight to execution. You've removed a guess, and removing guesses is the entire job of a good prompt.

"Instruction prompting... involves providing clear, specific and structured natural language instructions to a generative AI model... to guide them in performing complex tasks accurately and efficiently."

— GeeksforGeeks · Instruction Prompting · 2025
The Catch

A Verb Isn't A Whole Instruction

The most common failure is treating the verb as the finish line instead of the starting line. "Write an email" is better than "help me with my email," but it's still under-specified — the model still has to guess the tone, length, recipient, and goal. The verb removes the task ambiguity; constraints remove the shape ambiguity. GeeksforGeeks lists five components of an effective instruction, and the verb is only the first: clear task definition, contextual information, output format specification, tone and style, and length and detail. Lead with the verb, then add as many of the other four as the task needs.

Second catch: vague verbs are still vague. Not all verbs name a task with the same precision. "Improve this" and "fix this" are barely better than "help" — they don't tell the model what better looks like. The five workhorse verbs (Summarize, Write, Analyze, Compare, List) work because each implies a specific output shape. Reach for the verb that constrains, not the one that gestures.

Third catch — and this one is a genuine warning, not a style note: be careful what you instruct the model to do with sensitive data. Instruction prompting is powerful enough to do things like strip personal information from text, but Learn Prompting flags the risk directly: "Allowing AI to remove PII from text is a promising approach, but it should be used with extraordinary caution as it may make mistakes," and "you are sending PII to a third party (e.g. OpenAI if you are using ChatGPT), and these services may record and use your prompts for their own needs." A clear instruction will be followed faithfully — including when following it sends private data somewhere you didn't intend.

Fourth catch: the model extrapolates from your instruction, sometimes further than you meant. Learn Prompting's PII example notes the model "knows to replace `Cheap Dealz` with `[DEALERSHIP]` and `Jimmy Smith` with `[SALESPERSON]`, even though we did not explicitly tell it to do so." That generalization is usually a feature — but on a task where you need exact, bounded behavior, an instruction that's too open invites the model to improvise. Tighten the instruction until the room to improvise matches the room you actually want.

Real Use Cases

Where A Verb Earns Its Keystroke

The five verbs map onto the everyday tasks people actually use ChatGPT for. GeeksforGeeks catalogs the application domains — content creation, data processing, education, business automation, creative work — and Learn Prompting supplies the canonical worked examples. Here's where each verb does its best work:

  • Summarize → research, email triage, meeting notes — the highest-frequency verb. "Summarize this thread in three bullet points" turns a 40-message email chain into something you can act on in ten seconds. GeeksforGeeks' canonical form stacks a constraint: "Summarize the article in 100 words."
  • Write → drafting anything from scratch — emails, posts, outlines, replies. The verb that most rewards added constraints: "Write a 500-word blog post explaining how AI is transforming healthcare, focusing on diagnostics and patient care" (GeeksforGeeks) gives the model task + length + topic + focus in one line.
  • Analyze → evaluation and critique — when you want judgment, not a recap. Learn Prompting's essay-evaluation example is the template: "Read the following excerpt... provide feedback based on grammar, clarity, coherence, argument quality, and use of evidence. Provide a score from 1-10 for each attribute, along with reasoning." The verb plus criteria plus a scoring scale produces structured, defensible output.
  • Compare → decisions — anytime you're choosing between options. "Compare these two job offers across salary, growth, and commute" forces a side-by-side instead of two separate descriptions. The verb does the structuring for you.
  • List → anything you want to scan or save — the most save-worthy output shape, which is exactly why the brief bet on this video for Instagram. GeeksforGeeks' form even specifies the container: "List five pros and cons of electric vehicles in a table format." Verb + count + format = a screenshot-able answer.

A subtler use case worth its own mention: data transformation. Learn Prompting's name-parsing example shows instruction prompting handling format conversion with zero examples — given "we need it in this format '[Last name], [First name]'... convert the following name: John Smith," the model returns "Smith, John." The instruction is the program. No code, no examples — just a clearly stated transformation.

Common Mistake

Stopping At The Verb

The most common mis-application is treating the verb as the whole prompt. "Write an email" is genuinely better than "help me with my email" — but it's still leaving most of the model's accuracy on the table. The verb names the task; it doesn't name the shape. GeeksforGeeks' best-practice list leads with the fix: "Be specific and unambiguous — avoid vague instructions; precision helps the model understand the task better." Lead with the verb, then add the recipient, tone, length, and goal. The verb is the on-ramp, not the destination.

The second mistake is reaching for a gesturing verb instead of a commanding one. "Improve this," "fix this," "make this better" are verbs, but they don't name an output shape — the model still has to guess what "better" means. The workhorse five (Summarize, Write, Analyze, Compare, List) work precisely because each one implies a concrete result. If your verb could mean five different things, it's barely better than "help."

The third mistake is forgetting that a faithfully-followed instruction can do something you didn't intend. This is the flip side of the technique's power. Learn Prompting's caution is worth repeating: when you instruct a model to process sensitive text, "you are sending PII to a third party... and these services may record and use your prompts for their own needs." The model will obey a clear command exactly — including a command that sends private data somewhere it shouldn't go. Specificity cuts both ways.

// Takeaway

Start every prompt with a verb. Summarize, Write, Analyze, Compare, List — each one names a task the model was built to execute. "Help me with X" makes the model guess; a verb makes it act. That's the whole hack.

Then sharpen: verb first, then length, format, tone, and focus. The verb gets you 80% of the way from "trash" to "useful" in a single word; the constraints close the rest. It's the cheapest, most universal upgrade in the entire prompt-engineering toolkit — it costs nothing and applies to every prompt you'll ever write.

// Ryu's Read On The Data

This is the first time I'm writing this section as a win — on two of three platforms. V16 was the cleanest experiment the series has run (early queue, save-built, fast hook), and it landed every bet on Instagram and TikTok. YouTube stayed dead (5 views, 0% CTR even after a retitle) — but that's a title problem, not a content one, and the data section breaks it down. Instagram is the breakout I'd stopped expecting. 123 views at queue #1 (V14 got 27 at queue #10), and — the number that actually matters — 10 interactions in a single post when the entire prior run had produced one comment. 3 likes, 2 comments, 3 saves, 2 shares. The five-verb list got saved exactly where the brief said it would.

The hook bet won too. TikTok completion hit 1.2% — the first time the series ever cleared 1%, up from V14's 0.8%. V14's data told me the problem was at 0:02; V16's two-second contrast open is the proof of the fix. The wrinkle: average watch time actually fell (3.99s vs 4.38s). The fast hook doesn't make everyone watch longer — it splits the room into "watched it all" and "gone instantly," and kills the lukewarm middle. For an algorithm that rewards completion, that's the right trade.

And the one that still won't move: zero new followers, six videos deep. But V16 changes what that means. Before, no followers came with no engagement — same problem wearing two hats. Now the channel earns likes, comments, saves, shares… and converts none of it to a follow. That's not an interest problem anymore. It's a conversion problem, and it lives in the last two seconds — the gap between "that was useful" and actually tapping follow. That's the next thing I redesign.
