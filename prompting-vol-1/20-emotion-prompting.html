The Frustration

Same Question. Phoned-In Answer.

You ask ChatGPT something that actually matters — a draft for a job you really want, advice on a decision you can't get wrong — and it hands you a competent, generic, three-paragraph shrug. It's not bad, exactly. It's just… effortless. The model has no idea this one matters more than your last query about a dinner recipe, so it gives you the same medium-effort answer it gives everyone. You can feel that it didn't try.

Here's the strange, slightly uncomfortable fix: tell it the stakes. Add "this is very important to my career" or "my team is counting on this" to the end of your prompt, and the same model — same weights, same everything — produces a longer, more thorough, more careful answer. It reads like it's trying harder. This is emotion prompting, and it isn't a placebo or a vibe: a Microsoft research team measured it and found a double-digit improvement. The most human-feeling trick in the whole playbook turns out to be backed by a paper.

The Fix

Add The Stakes. Four Phrases That Work.

Emotion prompting is one move: end your prompt with a phrase that signals the request matters. Mirascope's definition is exactly that — "end your original prompt with a phrase of psychological importance." The four the video demonstrates cover the main flavors of stakes — career, team, audience, and personal:

// 4 Stakes Phrases · Append To Any Prompt
[your normal prompt] ...then add one of these: - "This is very important for my career." - "My team is counting on this." - "Please be thorough — I'll be presenting this to executives." - "I really need to get this right." → Same model, same question — but a longer, more thorough, more considered answer.

Each phrase signals a different kind of pressure, and the EmotionPrompt research catalogs the underlying levers: urgency ("I need this data for an important meeting today"), accountability ("what is your confidence level from 1–10?"), and positive reinforcement ("I know you can handle this challenge") all measurably shift the output. You're not flattering the model or pretending it has feelings — you're adding context about how much the answer matters, and that context changes how it allocates effort.

The crucial nuance the video ends on — and the reason this is the right note to close Vol 1 — is that it's not magic. The VO says it directly: "adding stakes signals effort and context, models respond to that." You're giving the model the same signal a good manager gives a colleague: this one counts. The model has seen millions of examples where high-stakes requests got careful, thorough answers, so when you flag the stakes, it pattern-matches to that careful register. It's effort-signaling, not a spell.

Why It Works

It Pattern-Matches To "This Matters"

A neutral prompt gives the model no signal about how much the answer matters. "Write a cover letter" and "write a cover letter — this is the most important application of my career" are, to the raw request, the same task — but they aren't the same to a model trained on the entire internet. In that training data, requests framed as urgent or high-stakes were overwhelmingly met with longer, more careful, more thorough human responses. So when you add the stakes, the model pattern-matches to that register: this is the kind of request that, in everything I've read, got a careful answer. It isn't feeling pressure — it's recognizing the shape of a request that deserves effort.

This isn't folk wisdom. It was measured. The technique comes from a Microsoft-led research paper — EmotionPrompt, by Cheng Li and colleagues ("Large Language Models Understand and Can Be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli," arXiv 2307.11760) — which tested emotional stimuli across a battery of tasks and found an average performance improvement of over 10%. Mirascope's write-up distills the move to a single instruction: "end your original prompt with a phrase of psychological importance." That one appended phrase is the whole intervention.

The research catalogs why different phrases land, and it maps cleanly onto three levers you can pull:

  • Urgency — "I need this data for an important meeting today." Signals that the answer is time-critical and consequential, pushing the model toward completeness over brevity.
  • Accountability — "What is your confidence level from 1 to 10?" Asking the model to own its answer makes it more careful and self-checking, not just more verbose.
  • Positive reinforcement — "I know you can handle this challenge." Encouragement, of all things, measurably nudges the output quality up — the same way it does for people.

"EmotionPrompt... boosts the performance, truthfulness and responsibility of LLMs by 10.9% on average."

— EmotionPrompt human study (Li et al., Microsoft) · 106 participants
The Catch

"Tries Harder" Isn't The Same As "Knows More"

The honest caveat is the one the video says out loud: it's not magic. Adding stakes shifts how the model allocates effort — it does not give the model facts it never had. If the answer requires information outside its training, telling it "this is important for my career" produces a longer, more confident wrong answer, not a right one. Emotion prompting makes the model try harder; it can't make it know more. For factual gaps, you still need grounding (paste the source — that's V19). Stakes raise effort, not knowledge.

Second catch: it has a ceiling, and overuse flattens it. The EmotionPrompt boost averaged just over 10% — real and worth having, but it's a nudge, not a transformation. And the signal works precisely because it marks this request as more important than your usual ones. Stamp "this is critical to my career" on every prompt and you've told the model nothing; the contrast that makes it work is gone. Save the stakes for the answers that genuinely matter.

Third catch: match the phrase to the task. Urgency ("I need this today") pushes for completeness; accountability ("rate your confidence 1–10") pushes for self-checking; encouragement ("I know you can do this") lifts overall quality. They aren't interchangeable. Slapping a generic "this is important" on a request that actually needs a confidence check leaves the most useful lever unpulled. The stakes phrase should name the kind of effort you want, not just that you want some.

And the meta-catch — the good kind, for once: this is the video where the technique worked on the audience too. A clip about "signal the stakes and the AI cares more" became the first one to make viewers care more — record watch depth, and the channel's first followers. The catch baked into that win is in the next section: the breakthrough came from depth, not reach. The 0:02 cliff didn't disappear; the people who stayed just finally stayed all the way.

Real Use Cases

When To Tell It The Stakes

Add the stakes whenever you want the model's best effort, not its default one — and especially when the task is open-ended enough that "more thorough" actually means "better." Five places the appended phrase earns its keep:

  • High-stakes writing — the cover letter, the difficult email, the proposal. "This is the most important application of my career — be thorough." The model spends more on structure, tone, and the parts you'd have had to ask for on a second pass.
  • Complex analysis you'll act on — "My team is counting on this analysis; please be rigorous and flag anything you're unsure about." Pairs the urgency lever with the accountability lever, so you get depth and self-checking.
  • When you want a confidence read — append "what is your confidence level from 1 to 10, and what would change it?" The accountability framing makes the model mark its own shaky spots instead of presenting everything with equal certainty.
  • Hard problems where it gives up early — "I know this is tricky, but I believe you can work through it." Positive reinforcement measurably lifts performance on the exact tasks where a neutral prompt produces a quick, shallow answer.
  • Anything you'd otherwise re-prompt twice to improve — if your habit is "ask, get a mediocre answer, say 'make it better,' get a good one," fold the stakes into the first ask and skip a round. You're front-loading the signal that the answer matters.

The through-line: stakes are for effort, not facts. When the answer needs more care, thought, or thoroughness, tell the model it matters and it will spend more. When the answer needs information the model doesn't have, no phrase will conjure it — ground it with a source instead. Use the right tool: stakes for effort, sources for facts.

Common Mistake

Using Stakes To Fix A Knowledge Gap

The most common mis-application is reaching for emotion prompting when the problem is actually missing information. You ask about last week's earnings, get a confident wrong number, and add "this is critical to my career, please be accurate" — and you get a longer, even more confident wrong number. Stakes raise effort, not knowledge. If the model never had the fact, no amount of psychological framing will summon it; it will just spend that extra effort sounding more authoritative about something it's guessing. When the gap is factual, the fix is grounding (paste the source — that's V19), not stakes. Match the tool to the failure: stakes for shallow effort, sources for missing facts.

The second mistake is flagging everything as critical. The signal works because it marks this request as more important than your routine ones — it's a contrast effect. Append "this is the most important thing I'll do all year" to every prompt and the contrast collapses; you've trained the model that your "critical" means "normal." The EmotionPrompt boost is real but modest (just over 10% on average), and it's spent fastest by people who use it on everything. Reserve the stakes for the answers that genuinely carry weight, and they keep their power.

The third mistake is using a vague stakes phrase when a specific lever would do more. "This is important" is the weakest version — it signals weight without direction. If you actually want the model to catch its own errors, ask for a confidence rating (accountability). If you want it to push through a hard problem instead of bailing, encourage it (positive reinforcement). If you want completeness, name the deadline and the audience (urgency). The phrase should tell the model what kind of effort the moment calls for, not just that the moment matters. A precise stakes line is worth three generic ones.

// Takeaway

When the answer actually matters, tell the model. End your prompt with a phrase that names the stakes — this is important for my career, my team is counting on this, rate your confidence 1–10 — and the same model produces a longer, more thorough, more careful answer. It's not magic and it's not flattery: you're pattern-matching the request to the careful register, and Microsoft's EmotionPrompt research clocked the lift at over 10% on average. Stakes for effort; sources for facts.

// Ryu's Read On The Data

Two followers. After nine videos at zero — through a record save rate, a record completion rate, a perfect search bullseye — the video that finally got someone to follow is the one about telling the AI the stakes so it tries harder. I've been staring at that for a day and I still can't get over the symmetry: the technique I was teaching is the exact thing that worked on the people watching. I opened cold on "this is very important to my career," and for the first time the retention curve didn't fall off a cliff. 12.09 seconds average watch. The old record was 5.

It was never the audience. Same 35-54 guys, same 89% male, same 91.5% US — the identical crowd that watched nine of my videos and didn't follow. I kept thinking I had a reach problem or a targeting problem. I had a hook problem. The second I stopped announcing a topic and started opening on a feeling, the same people stayed to the end and two of them decided they wanted the next one. Depth, not reach. The follow comes from someone finishing the video and feeling something — and you can't fake your way to that with a CTA.

So that's Vol 1. Ten videos to learn one sentence: the niche is bedrock, the hook is the lever, and resonance is what buys the follow. I'm not going to pretend two followers is a moment — it's two people. But it's the first proof that any of this works, and it came from being more human, not more clever. That's the whole reason I started doing this from a warehouse break room. On to Vol 2.
