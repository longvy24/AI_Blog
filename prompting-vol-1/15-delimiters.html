The Frustration

ChatGPT Keeps Misreading You

You paste a long article into ChatGPT and ask "summarize this." The reply summarizes the wrong section — or worse, it answers the literal phrase "summarize this" instead of the article. You try again. Same problem. The output keeps feeling off in a way that doesn't reduce to "the model is bad." It's that the model couldn't tell where your instruction ended and your data began.

That's the failure mode delimiters solve. The fix isn't a better prompt; it's a structural one. Wrap your pasted content in a recognizable boundary — triple quotes, hashtag fences, or XML tags — and ChatGPT stops blending the two. The accuracy lift shows up on the very next prompt, with no other change. This is the smallest formatting fix in the entire prompt-engineering toolkit, and it's the one most casually skipped.

The Fix

Three Symbols. Pick One. Stay Consistent.

A delimiter is whatever boundary you put around your data to tell the model "this part is content, not instruction." Three options dominate practical use, and they cover almost every paste-in scenario:

// The 3-Delimiter Toolkit
Option 1 — Triple quotes (best for prose / pasted articles): Summarize the text enclosed by triple quotes: """ [paste the article here] """ Option 2 — Hash fences (best for short content blocks): ### Article ### [paste the article here] ### End ### Summarize the article above. Option 3 — XML tags (best for structured / multi-section inputs): <article> [paste the article here] </article> <task>Summarize the article in three bullet points.</task>

Triple quotes are the lowest-friction default. Kirenz's Gen AI & Prompting course frames them as "a simple yet effective way to enclose blocks of text within your prompt — particularly useful for setting apart content that needs to be processed separately from your instructions." A practical detail the same source flags: "ensure there's a line break before and after the enclosed text for better readability and to prevent any confusion with the surrounding instructions."

Hash fences and XML tags are stronger when the input has structure you want to label. Vieira's Medium reference on delimiters covers eight options total — including triple backticks for code, dashes, pipes, and curly braces — but converges on the same architectural advice: "Use the same delimiter consistently for the same type of information or task to avoid confusion."

For a definition that's tighter than most: Kirenz writes that delimiters are "special markers or symbols used to separate and define different sections of text or data. In the context of prompts for generative AI, delimiters serve as clear boundaries between instructions, content to be processed, and expected outputs."

"Three delimiter options — triple quotes, hash marks, or XML tags — pick one and stay consistent."

Why It Works

Boundaries Beat Prose Hints

Without delimiters, ChatGPT has to guess where your instruction ends and your data begins. Most of the time the guess is correct — the data is short, the instruction is at the top, and the model's training has seen this shape before. But the failure modes show up exactly when the data gets long, when the data looks like an instruction (questions, imperatives, anything in the second person), or when the instruction is buried after the data instead of in front of it. A delimiter eliminates the guess. The boundary is now explicit.

Kirenz's Gen AI & Prompting course frames the four mechanisms delimiters deliver:

  • Clarity — "They provide clear boundaries between different parts of a prompt, making it easier for the AI to understand the structure of the input."
  • Separation of concerns — "Delimiters allow you to separate instructions from the content to be processed, reducing ambiguity."
  • Structured output — "By using delimiters in your prompt to specify the desired output format, you can guide the AI to provide responses in a consistent, structured manner."
  • Error reduction — "Clear delimitation reduces the chances of the AI misinterpreting parts of the prompt or mixing instructions with content."

The mechanism is structural, not stylistic. A delimited prompt isn't politer to the model; it's clearer to the parser inside the model that decides "what's the user asking?" versus "what's the user giving me?" That parsing step happens before any reasoning. If it lands wrong, every downstream token is contaminated.

XML tags are particularly strong on the structured-input side because of how modern models are trained. The Kirenz course notes that XML tags carry "semantic clarity — tags can be named descriptively, providing context about the enclosed content," and that "many AI models are trained on web content, making them adept at interpreting XML-like structures." A <article> tag isn't just a fence — it's a hint to the model that what follows is article-shaped content.

"Delimiters are special markers or symbols used to separate and define different sections of text or data. In the context of prompts for generative AI, delimiters serve as clear boundaries between instructions, content to be processed, and expected outputs."

— Jan Kirenz · Gen AI & Prompting · 2024
The Catch

Pick One. Don't Stack Three.

The most common failure mode is using too many different delimiters in the same prompt. Vieira's reference on delimiters states it plainly: "Too many different delimiters can confuse both the AI and the user. Stick to a few types that serve your needs well." A prompt with triple quotes, hashtags, dashes, AND XML tags doesn't read as "carefully structured" to the model — it reads as noise. The clarity benefit collapses past two delimiter types in a single prompt.

Second catch: inconsistency across a workflow. If your Round 1 prompt wraps data in """, and your Round 2 prompt switches to ###, the model can sometimes carry the structural assumption from Round 1 into Round 2 and produce confused output. Vieira's rule: "Use the same delimiter consistently for the same type of information or task to avoid confusion." Pick one style at the start of a workflow and hold it.

Third catch: delimiter collisions with the content itself. If the article you're pasting contains literal triple quotes (a transcript with quoted dialogue, for example), using triple quotes as your delimiter creates an ambiguous parse. Vieira's escape-character recommendation is the safest fix, but the cheaper fix is to pick a delimiter your content won't contain — XML tags with descriptive names (<article>, <transcript>) rarely collide with anything.

Fourth catch: over-engineering simple prompts. Kirenz's course is explicit about when the technique is and isn't worth the keystrokes: "As the complexity of your digital marketing tasks increases, the importance of using appropriate delimiters becomes more pronounced. While simple tasks may not require extensive use of delimiters, incorporating them as a habit will prepare you for more complex scenarios." A two-line prompt doesn't need a delimiter. A 500-word paste does.

Real Use Cases

Where Delimiters Earn Their Keystrokes

Five paste-in patterns where delimiters lift output quality immediately. Each one maps to a documented application in the source references — the Kirenz course catalogs five digital marketing applications in particular; UVic's prompt-design library guide frames the same applications more generically.

  • Summarize a long article or transcript — the canonical case. Wrap the pasted text in """ triple quotes and put the instruction before the wrap. Kirenz's example: "Analyze the following product description and suggest three catchy social media posts for Instagram. Ensure each post is no longer than 280 characters." followed by the delimited paste.
  • Side-by-side comparison of two inputs — when you want to compare A/B variants, XML tags labeled by content win. Wrap each option in its own named tag (<variant_a> and <variant_b>) so the model can refer back to each one by name in its analysis.
  • Code blocks for review or refactor — Vieira specifically calls out triple backticks here: "Particularly useful for code snippets or formatted text." The triple-backtick fence is recognized natively by ChatGPT's markdown renderer, so the output preserves formatting cleanly.
  • Structured input for content briefs — when the input itself has sub-sections (target audience, product details, tone, constraints), nested XML tags keep each section addressable. Kirenz's nested-delimiter example pairs <target_audience> with triple quotes inside, so the AI gets both the type label and the content boundary in one structure.
  • Specifying the output format you want back — wrap your desired output shape in tags. Kirenz's social-post example asks the model to format its reply inside <instagram_post> and <twitter_post> tags so the response is parseable downstream.

Common Mistake

Mixing Delimiters Inside The Same Prompt

The most common mis-application is using two or three different delimiter types in the same prompt — wrapping the article in triple quotes, the instructions in hashtags, and the desired output format in XML tags, all at once. It feels like careful structure. It reads to the model as inconsistent. Vieira's rule is the cleanest test: "Use the same delimiter consistently for the same type of information or task to avoid confusion." Pick one delimiter for "data I'm pasting in" and use it every time.

The second mistake is treating delimiters like decoration — wrapping every line of the prompt in something, including the actual instruction. Delimiters only carry information when they signal a boundary. If everything has fences around it, nothing has a boundary. Reserve delimiters for the content you want the model to process as data, not instruction.

The third mistake — flagged by the UVic Libraries' Prompt Design For Beginners guide — is forgetting that delimiters can be "a descriptive word (eg. Title:)", not just a symbol. If your delimiter """ shows up inside the article you're pasting, switch to a label-based delimiter instead. Article: followed by content, followed by Instruction: is a viable fallback when symbol-based delimiters collide with the content itself.

// Takeaway

Three options. Pick one — """ for prose, ### for short blocks, XML tags for structured input — and stay consistent inside any single prompt. ChatGPT stops confusing instructions with content. Output quality lifts immediately, on the very next paste-in, with no other change.

The delimiter becomes a habit, not a tactic. Build the muscle: every time you paste anything longer than two lines into ChatGPT, wrap it. The cost is six keystrokes. The gain is one less round of "actually, summarize this part."
