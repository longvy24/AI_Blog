10 Ways to Use AI at Work
Without Leaking

The company I work for had exactly one rule about AI: use whatever you want — never put company data in it. Simple to say, harder to follow when the thing you need help with is the sensitive thing. These are the ten habits I built.

R
Ryu Vy
AIToolsDaily24 · By a human, for humans

For a while the policy where I work was remarkably relaxed. Use any AI you like — any model, any provider, whatever gets the job done. There was one condition attached, and it was one line long.

The entire policy
Use whatever you want.
Never put company data in it.

That rule is easy to agree with and genuinely awkward to follow. The problems worth asking an AI about are the specific ones — this query is slow, this file has a bug, this spreadsheet won't reconcile. The specifics are exactly what you're not allowed to hand over.

So you learn to separate the two. What you actually need from the model is almost never the data — it's the reasoning about the shape of the problem. Once that clicks, most of these habits follow naturally. Here are the ten I ended up using every day.

The Ten

Every Prompt, Copy-Paste Ready

01 / 10Reframe
Generic Twin
"Here's a generic version of my problem: [describe the shape, no names]. What causes it?"
The single highest-leverage habit here. Describe the structure of your problem with every identifying detail removed and the answer is just as good — because the structure was the part you needed help with. "Our billing service times out when two jobs claim the same record" becomes "a scheduling system deadlocks when two workers claim the same slot." Same diagnosis, nothing leaked.
02 / 10Data
Synthetic Rows
"Generate 20 fake rows for this schema: [columns and types]. Realistic, no real people."
Never paste the real table. Hand over the schema — column names and types — and ask for invented rows. You get data that behaves exactly like yours for testing and debugging, and belongs to nobody. This is also the fix for the most common accidental leak there is: pasting a spreadsheet with real customer names in it because you only cared about column C.
03 / 10Masking
Placeholder Tokens
"Treat [CLIENT_A] and [SYSTEM_1] as placeholders. I'll substitute the real values after."
When you can't avoid naming things, name them something else. Models reason about [CLIENT_A] exactly as well as about a real company — the label was never doing any work. Keep a consistent set of tokens so the answer is still readable when you swap the real values back in. Say the substitution out loud in the prompt or it will sometimes try to guess what the placeholders stand for.
04 / 10Scope
Ask the Category
"How does a system like this usually handle [problem]? General patterns only."
Sometimes you don't need help with your instance at all — you need to know how the category works, then apply it yourself. Ask about the general pattern and nothing of yours crosses the wire, not even a redacted version. This is the safest prompt on the list and it solves more than you'd expect, because most problems at work are a common pattern wearing local clothes.
05 / 10Code
Strip Before You Paste
"Here's the function with keys and hostnames removed. The bug is in the logic:"
API keys, internal endpoints, database hostnames and auth tokens are never where the bug is. Delete them before you paste — replace with API_KEY and internal.host. You lose nothing diagnostically and you stop shipping credentials into a chat log. Worth doing even with an approved tool, because pasted secrets have a way of ending up in screenshots later.
06 / 10Logs
Retype the Error
"The error line is: 'connection refused on retry'. What causes that?"
Log dumps are the most underrated leak on this list. A stack trace carries internal hostnames, file paths, user IDs, session tokens and sometimes the actual payload — and you pasted 200 lines to ask about one. Type the line that matters. The friction is the point: retyping forces you to read what you were about to send.
07 / 10Split
Split the Task
"Draft the generic logic. I'll wire it to the real data in the approved tool."
Most work divides cleanly into a thinking half and a sensitive half. Do the thinking wherever the best model is, then do the wiring where the data lives. Nothing has to cross. This one also survives policy changes gracefully — when the rules tighten, you shift where the boundary sits instead of relearning how you work.
08 / 10Memory
Kill the History
Settings → Data controls → history & training: OFF. Start a fresh chat for anything work-adjacent.
Not a prompt — a setting, and the one people skip. Anything you paste can outlive the conversation you pasted it into. Turn history and training off by default for work, and start clean sessions rather than letting one long thread accumulate context about your job. A single chat that remembers everything is a single chat that can reveal everything.
09 / 10Drift
Stay Anonymous
"Don't reference my company, role or location in your answer. Keep it neutral."
You rarely leak in one dramatic paste. You leak by accumulation — a job title here, a city there, an industry term, a tool only three companies use. Individually harmless, collectively an identity. Telling the model to keep its answers generic stops it echoing those details back and quietly compounding them through a conversation.
10 / 10Audit
Audit Yourself
"From this whole chat, what could you guess about my employer? Be specific."
The one almost nobody runs, and the reason to save this post. Ask the model to profile you from your own conversation. If it can name your industry, your role, or your region, you've been leaking without noticing — and now you know exactly which details did it. Run it once a week on a long-running thread. It's a two-second check that turns an abstract policy into something you can actually measure.
// The one that changes behaviour

Number 10 is the only item here with a feedback loop. The other nine are habits you either follow or don't — you can't tell from the outside. The self-audit is the one that shows you, in the model's own words, how much of your working life you've handed over. Most people are surprised the first time.

The Series

Five Eras, None of Them Mine

This is the first unit of a new series about something I've never seen anyone publish honestly: what happens to you when your AI tools keep changing and you don't get a vote. The open era above was stage one. Then the company built its own tooling. Then external platforms were banned outright. Then licences started getting bought. Then the stack changed again and I had to learn a new editor from scratch.

Five stages, in order, none of them my decision. That's not an unusual career — it's roughly the path every company is walking right now, and most people are living somewhere in the middle of it. Every unit ships with prompts you can steal, because the story is what makes you watch and the prompts are what make you save it.
