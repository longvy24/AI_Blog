The Tool

It's Perplexity AI

The video title says "Stop Using Google — Try This AI Search Tool" without naming the tool. The tool is Perplexity AI. It's named in the script at the 10-second mark and visible in the video frames. Unlike Video 1 where the title was the problem, here the title is deliberately vague — a curiosity hook designed to make viewers watch long enough to find out what the tool is.

Perplexity AI is an AI-powered answer engine. Instead of returning a list of links like a traditional search engine, it reads the web in real time and returns a synthesized answer with cited sources. You can ask follow-up questions in the same thread. It remembers what you asked. The result is closer to talking to a research assistant than typing keywords into a box.

"Google gives you 10 blue links and 4 ads. You still have to click, read, and figure it out yourself. That was fine in 2015."

Accuracy Check

The Pricing Problem

The closing section of the video shows a screen with "Standard Plan - $29/month" — a pricing figure that doesn't match any actual Perplexity plan. The script also says "It's free to start" — which is accurate, but having both statements in the same video is confusing.

The actual pricing as of May 2026: Perplexity has a free tier that gives you unlimited basic searches with citations — genuinely useful on its own. The paid plans are Perplexity Pro at $17/month (billed annually) which adds access to top AI models including Gemini 3.1 Pro, Sonar 2, and Claude Sonnet 4.6, and Perplexity Max at $167/month for highest usage and advanced reasoning models. The $29/month figure shown in the video doesn't exist.

This is another case of Keyvello generating plausible-looking content with incorrect specifics. A viewer who paused on that pricing frame and went to perplexity.ai would find the numbers don't match. That's a trust problem — and exactly the kind of thing the review step in the workflow is supposed to catch.

The Real Tool

What Perplexity AI Actually Does

Despite the visual and pricing issues, the core concept in the video is accurate. Perplexity AI does give you direct, cited answers instead of a list of links. Here's when it genuinely outperforms Google:

  • Research questions where you'd normally open 5+ tabs — Perplexity synthesizes them into one answer with sources you can verify
  • Comparing products, tools, or options — instead of reading 10 reviews, you get a structured comparison with citations
  • Following up on a topic in the same thread — it remembers the context of your previous questions
  • Fact-checking something — the citations are right there, no separate verification step needed
  • Current events — it reads the live web, not a training dataset with a cutoff date

Where Google still wins: finding specific websites by name, local results, shopping, and anything where you want the actual page rather than a summary.

Independent Research

Perplexity vs Google: What The Data Shows

Beyond the video's claims, there's useful independent research on where Perplexity actually wins and loses. Two real-world comparisons — one from CodeX on Medium by product manager Mike Lyngaas, and one from PCMag by tech writer Alan Bradley who switched exclusively to Perplexity for a full week — give a much more complete picture than a 60-second short can.

Where both sources agree Perplexity wins: research, summarization, and complex queries. The PCMag reviewer found Perplexity far superior for synthesizing complex scientific studies, sourcing academic-only results, and providing inline numbered citations for every data point — making it easy to verify claims and dig deeper. The CodeX comparison found the same pattern: for "How do I X" and "Summarize X" queries, Perplexity consistently delivered faster, more structured answers with relevant sources surfaced automatically.

Where Google still wins: reviews, criticism, product searches, and exploratory browsing. The PCMag reviewer specifically noted that getting subjective reviewer opinions out of Perplexity required forcing it to behave like a link aggregator — clumsy in a chatbot interface. The CodeX comparison found Perplexity struggled with resource retrieval — when asked for local movie showtimes, it listed theater names but couldn't surface actual times even after multiple follow-ups.

The hallucination caveat is real. The PCMag reviewer caught Perplexity hallucinating twice in a week — once misreporting a drone's altitude ceiling, and once citing a statistic from a real study that didn't contain that specific data. Citations help you catch these, but they don't eliminate the risk. Always verify important claims.

The most useful framing from the CodeX piece: Perplexity is like the assertive senior member of your team — succinct and well-structured, but occasionally overconfident and wrong with little room for iteration. Google is the hungry junior analyst — exhaustive, but shows too much work without delivering the "so what." The video's claim to "stop using Google" is an oversimplification. The more accurate version: use Perplexity for research and synthesis, keep Google for everything else.

The Data Story

Why This One Underperformed

Video 2 got significantly fewer views than Video 1 across every platform. YouTube: 266 vs 1,002. TikTok: 103 vs 298. Instagram: 75 vs 131. The topic is relevant, the hook is strong, but the numbers dropped. Here's what I think happened.

The hook is aggressive but vague. "Stop Googling things" is a bold claim that creates friction — some viewers will keep watching out of curiosity, but others will scroll past because it feels like an exaggerated promise. Video 1's hook ("Did you know Claude can talk to your Google Drive?") was specific and surprising. Video 2's hook is more confrontational than curious.

TikTok dropped at 0:02. The analytics show most viewers left at the two-second mark — even earlier than Video 1's 0:01 drop. The AI presenter format may be playing a role here. A realistic-looking human avatar that doesn't move quite right can trigger an uncanny valley response — something feels slightly off and viewers scroll away before consciously understanding why.

0% full video completion on TikTok means not a single viewer watched the whole thing. That's a retention signal that tells the algorithm this content doesn't hold attention, which limits further distribution.
