// THE FIRST TEN, HONESTLY
The Setup

Ten Videos. Mostly AI.

Every video in this series — 01 through 10 — was generated by Keyvello from an RTFC-structured prompt. AI script, AI voiceover (via embedded ElevenLabs), AI visuals, AI captions, AI assembly. My job is supposed to be: review the output, catch the errors, ship it. The audit below is what happens when that review loop is loose.

Across ten videos, I've documented 30+ production errors — hallucinated product names, wrong prices, audio defects, misspelled hook frames, outros pointing to accounts that aren't mine. Two videos broke out anyway. The other eight cratered at the first second. This is the post where I write that pattern down.

What Worked

The Two Breakouts

Two videos in the series broke 1,000 views. V01 "Google Drive AI" hit 1,002 on YouTube in 48 hours and added 298 TikTok views and 131 Instagram views — 1,431 total. V07 "Copilot Cowork" hit 1,070 on TikTok with an 11.34-second average watch time (the series best on completion) and pulled in another 129 across YouTube and Instagram for 1,199 total.

Neither was clean. V01 had a TikTok audio defect — the platform audio bled in, killing the first second of retention. TikTok analytics literally flagged it: "most viewers stopped watching at 0:01." V07 had four documented errors including an outro frame that pointed followers to @TechVisualsPro, a completely different account. Both videos still broke out. The lesson: topic demand outran execution failure. Google Drive AI had search demand. Microsoft Copilot inside a 45–54 audience had urgent relevance. The algorithm pushed both despite the production mess.

AI Errors

What Keyvello Got Wrong

Keyvello generated the entire pipeline — script, voiceover, visuals, captions, assembly — for all 10 videos. It's a powerful tool. It's also a sloppy one. Across the series, the recurring failure modes were:

  • Hallucinated products. V03 called the featured tool "Perplexity Computer" — a product that doesn't exist. The blog post initially researched it as real before catching the error.
  • Wrong prices, repeatedly. V02 said Perplexity Pro was $29/month (it's $17). V06 had the on-screen frame say "$29" while the voiceover said "$10" for Midjourney (basic is $10). V08 called Gamma "free forever" — there's a one-time 400-credit cap.
  • Misspelled hook frames. V08's first frame, the one that decides whether anyone keeps watching, read "Qaurterly Sals Report" — two misspellings burned into second 0:00. TikTok drop-off was 0:02.
  • Audio defects. V01 had platform audio bleed on TikTok. V05 came out in mono, audible from only one speaker — same Keyvello settings as every other video, different result.
  • Wrong outros. V05 outro pointed to @AIToolsDaily (missing the "24"). V07 pointed to a real-but-unrelated account, @TechVisualsPro. V08 used a fake "GAMMA" Follow button with no real handle visible at all.
  • Product never named. V09 is a video about Clipdrop. The word "Clipdrop" never appears in the video. No logo, no URL, no identifiable interface. Anyone curious enough to click had nowhere to go.
  • Wrong dates and unsupported claims. V07 said Copilot Cowork "launched this week" — GA was three weeks earlier. V10's calendar frame showed October 2023 in a May 2026 video. V04 claimed ElevenLabs and CapCut were used as separate tools — neither was; Keyvello did everything.

"Keyvello is a powerful tool. It's also a sloppy one. The job isn't to use less of it — it's to add the review loop it doesn't have."

Human Errors

What I Got Wrong

A fair amount of the damage in the first 10 videos was mine, not Keyvello's. The errors I can't blame on the model:

  • V01's title was AI-generated and shipped without review. "Google Drive AI" is the framing that the blog post had to spend its first three paragraphs correcting. The topic survived. The framing didn't help.
  • V03's blog post was fabricated before I caught it. When Keyvello hallucinated "Perplexity Computer," my first draft of the blog post researched that non-existent product and wrote a breakdown of features it doesn't have. The corrected post is what's live now.
  • V09's title got switched mid-flight. I changed it after posting to something more algorithm-friendly, then reverted to the original "Stop Using Photoshop — This AI Edits in 1 Click." That's not a CTA. That's a dead end. Anyone arriving from a search query had no next step.
  • V10's analytics submission was the wrong file. I uploaded the wrong YouTube zip to the dashboard. The TikTok screenshots I attached were from V09. Instagram data wasn't captured at all. The numbers in this audit for V10 are partial because of me.
The Pattern

Second 0:01 Decides Everything

Six of the ten TikToks had viewer drop-off at second 0:01 or 0:02. That isn't an algorithm problem. The algorithm is doing its job — it's serving the videos to the For You page (91% on average across the series). It's the hook that's failing. Bad audio kills 0:01. A misspelled frame kills 0:01. A confusing AI-presenter face kills 0:01. The viewer's thumb is already moving.

The two breakouts both had hooks that survived the first two seconds. V01's "Google Drive AI" had concrete-noun + curiosity in the opening frame. V07's "Breaking News" template on Microsoft Copilot landed because the audience was older, work-focused, and already context-loaded. Everywhere else, the first frame did the work — and lost.

The Playbook

A New Series — Same Lessons

Videos 11–20 aren't another "what tool today" feed. They're a single connected series on one craft topic, with the entire upload playbook re-built around what the first 10 made obvious. Topic kept under wraps — but every strategic change below is locked in.

  • A connected series, not a daily feed. Same niche, same audience, episode logic — each video assumes you watched the last one. The first 10 burned reach by feeling like a random tool-of-the-day stream; that posture is gone.
  • No insider jargon in titles. Ever. "Midjourney V8.1" and "OpenAI Prism" proved viewers scroll past terms they don't recognize in half a second. Every new title leads with a brand viewers already know, paired with a specific outcome they can picture. Jargon gets demoted to the description body where it helps SEO without killing CTR.
  • Six title patterns, rotating. Five of the first 10 used the same "Stop using X" pattern and the last two got 0% CTR on 100+ impressions. The new series cycles through question, number, story, instruction, secret-reveal, and experiment formats — so the channel page stops reading like one repeated ad.
  • TikTok becomes the primary platform. Same first-10 content delivered ~30% more views on TikTok than YouTube, with the curve still accelerating (last 5 days vs. first 5: TikTok up 6.9×, YouTube down 71%). The new series posts to TikTok first and treats YouTube as secondary distribution until CTR recovers.
  • Cadence is now different per platform. YouTube: one video every 2–3 days (daily underperformers compound channel-level damage). TikTok: daily (recency is itself a ranking signal during the 7–10 day algorithm warmup). Instagram: 3–4 per week (daily posting reads as spam-adjacent and suppresses reach).
  • Captions optimize for what each platform actually rewards. TikTok ranks comments heaviest — every caption now ends with a one-word-reply question. Instagram ranks saves heaviest — every caption ends with "Save this 🔖". YouTube descriptions front-load the search-friendly phrases the titles deliberately avoid.
  • The Instagram bio gets rewritten before video 11 posts. 23 profile visits, 2 follows in the first 10 is a 91% leak rate — the single highest-leverage fix in the data. New bio names what the channel posts and how often, and a follow CTA is baked into the last 2 seconds of every Reel.
  • Each platform gets its own independent test. Three of the first 10 videos that flopped on YouTube were Instagram's top performers. No video gets killed because one platform rejected it — each gets its own week to find its audience.
  • Cross-linking from the existing hits. Every new video ships with a pinned comment on V01 "Google Drive AI" (1,002 views, still being served impressions) and on the May 12 TikTok (646 views, the platform's breakout) — funneling residual traffic from the wins of the first 10 into the new series.
// The One Thing To Remember

The first ten videos cost 30+ documented errors to ship 4,407 views. Two broke out anyway because topic demand was strong enough to outrun the execution problems. The other eight cratered at second 0:01. The fix isn't more AI. The fix is the review loop AI doesn't have — and putting it in front of every video before it goes live.
