// THE REAL STORY
Background

Not An AI Expert. An AI User.

My name is Ryu Vy — that's an alias, but the person behind it is real. I'm a full-time project manager at one of the largest EV distribution centers on the East Coast. I have an associates in Computer Technology from a community college back in my hometown, a bachelor's in Information Science Technology from Penn State, and I'm currently working toward a second bachelor's in Software Development and Analytics at Purdue Global — paid for by my company.

I'm not a researcher. I'm not a Silicon Valley engineer. I'm someone who works a demanding full-time job, goes to school on the side, and has been quietly using AI tools to make both of those things more manageable for a few years now. That's the perspective I'm writing from.

The Journey

How I Got Here

Where it started
Like a lot of people, I first started using AI to help with school. ChatGPT became my study partner — checking answers, helping me structure discussion posts and papers where I'd provide the information, the sources, and my own voice. It cut down the time I spent writing, proofreading, and working through complex problems.
At work
At my day job, I started using AI to fix errors in the PowerApps I'd built for my building, untangle complex Excel formulas, write VBA, SQL, and Python for operational tools. Things that used to take hours got done in minutes. Then my company banned external AI access — now we use internal AI or go through a formal API request process to use tools like Claude or Gemini through VS Code.
The Reddit post that changed things
I came across this post on r/passive_income from someone running faceless YouTube and TikTok channels using AI tools — scripts, voiceovers, images, and full video generation, no camera or editing skills needed. They broke down exactly how it worked and what it was actually generating. It wasn't life-changing money — but it made more than it cost.

The post got 440 upvotes and 162 comments. A lot of the comments were skeptical. But one comment stood out to me — someone pointing out that there are tons of videos and content about using AI to make content, but almost nobody actually shows you how to do it step by step. That comment is a big part of why this blog exists the way it does. I'm not just making AI content. I'm documenting exactly how I make it.

I figured: I'm already paying for Claude Max. What do I have to lose?

The experiment
I picked up a Keyvello Pro subscription and set up two separate content channels simultaneously: one for AI tech content (this one), one for Reddit story content. The Reddit channel flopped fast. YouTube was essentially zero views — the only plays were me checking on the video. Instagram got some traction but nothing meaningful, maybe 100-200 views if a video was lucky. TikTok was slightly better but in the same range. Nothing came close to what the AI tech videos were doing. I shut down that experiment early and put everything into this channel.
Right now
Ten videos in. A website live. A newsletter connected. A thumbnail generator I built. A trend analyzer I deployed on Vercel. All of it built with AI — and all of it documented here.

"I figured: I'm already paying for Claude Pro. What do I have to lose? So I tried it."

The Purpose

Why This Blog Exists

Three reasons, honestly.

First: I work in a place where AI is being integrated into everyday operations right now. Not theoretically — actually. I've watched people get left behind by tools they didn't understand and couldn't keep up with. I don't want that to happen to people who are willing to learn but just don't know where to start.

Second: I wanted to test whether you could build a content channel almost entirely with AI and have it be legitimate — not spam, not low-effort, but actually useful content that a real person built using real AI tools in a real workflow. This blog is part of that experiment.

Third: side income — and full documentation of how I got there. The Reddit post that inspired this said AI content generation was the most profitable side hustle they tested. I want to find out if that's true. But beyond the money, I want to document every step I took to make this happen — the tools, the costs, the time invested, the workflows I built, the things that failed, and the things that worked. If you've ever wanted to try something like this and didn't know where to start, this blog is meant to be the roadmap I wish I had. I'll share everything, including if it fails.

The Workflow

How a Video Gets Made

Every piece of content starts with a system I built — not a template I downloaded, but an actual workflow I designed with Claude's help and then iterated on until it worked. Here's how it flows:

1
Trend Analysis
A trend analyzer I built and deployed on Vercel identifies what AI topics are getting traction right now across TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube. It has 60+ pre-built search terms and returns sentiment analysis, top topics, and platform-specific hashtags for each query. I check it, pick a topic, and move to the next step. One ongoing improvement I'm working on: making it pull a rolling list of new topics each time it runs so I'm not seeing the same results twice.
2
Prompt Generation
I use an RFTC model (Role, Format, Task, Context) I built with Claude as the base structure for every Keyvello video prompt. The same framework also powers my thumbnail generator and trend analysis prompts. I provide the topic and context — the system generates the prompt format. Claude also builds out a full production brief: hook, Keyvello settings, full script, captions, and hashtags for all three platforms — everything I need to paste directly into Keyvello.
3
Video Generation
Keyvello Pro ($100/month) takes the prompt and generates the short-form video. I review it, check the facts, and decide if it goes up as-is or needs a re-run with a refined prompt. Most videos go through at least two prompt iterations. Each generated video also gets a critique run through Claude — graded on hook strength, visual quality, captions, music balance, CTA, and platform readiness — before it gets posted. For the AI Prompting series specifically, I used HeyGen HyperFrames to generate the text-based shorts — a different visual style from the Keyvello videos, leaning into clean typography and on-screen text rather than AI-generated imagery.
4
Thumbnail
A custom thumbnail generator I built (v3, currently) takes the video title and generates a branded thumbnail matching the channel's lime-green-on-dark visual identity. I select the icon and text — the tool handles the rest. This tool exists because of analysis. After reviewing early video performance, Claude flagged that none of my thumbnails made the videos uniquely identifiable to my channel. The thumbnail generator was built specifically to fix that problem.
5
Blog Post
Claude drafts the expanded blog post for each video. I review every word — correcting anything fabricated, adding sources, adjusting the voice, and making sure what gets published is something I'd actually stand behind. The blog post includes platform performance data in a sidebar so readers can see how each video actually performed across YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram.
6
Distribution
Videos go to YouTube Shorts, TikTok, and Instagram Reels simultaneously. The platform strategy is different for each: TikTok is the fast virality engine, YouTube is long-term SEO compounding, and Instagram is the evergreen brand builder. One idea, three platforms, one session.
7
Analysis Retrospective
This is the step that actually makes everything else better over time. After each video cycle, I pull the analytics from all three platforms and feed them into Claude for a full performance review: what topics performed best on which platform, what hook formats drove the most early retention, where the drop-off happened, what the data says about the next video's direction. This is also how I build new tools — every tool in this workflow was born from something the analysis flagged as a gap. The thumbnail generator came from analysis. The production brief format came from analysis. The goal is engagement, shares, follows, and eventually monetization — and the only way to get there is to treat every video as a data point and actually learn from it.

// A Note On "Free" AI Tools

I watched a short that listed several AI video tools as "free" — including some of the ones I tried early on. That was misleading at best. Almost everything requires tokens, and free tiers hide quality outputs behind a paywall. The number of videos you can generate is capped by how many tokens you have, which often means one video per prompt with no room to iterate without paying more.

HeyGen HyperFrames was the only tool in that video that was genuinely free — because it runs as a skill inside Claude using tokens you've already paid for. If you have a Claude subscription, there's no additional cost. I had Claude install HyperFrames for me directly, the same way I've installed a number of other skills from skills.sh. That's one of the best things about working with AI APIs — you can have them do a lot of the heavy lifting, including setting themselves up. There will be a dedicated video on agents and skills that goes deeper on this, but the short version is: Claude can install its own tools, and that changes how much you can actually get done without being a developer.

Everything else in this stack has a real price attached to it, and I'll be transparent about what things actually cost as this experiment continues.
