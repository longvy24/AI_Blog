The Setup

Ten Videos. One Connected Series.

Vol 1 — videos 11 through 20 — is the answer to everything the First 10 got wrong. Gone is Keyvello's one-shot AI pipeline; every video here is hand-built with HeyGen HyperFrames + Claude Code Max, GSAP animation, Kokoro voiceover, and a Whisper QA pass on every render. Gone is the random tool-of-the-day feed; this is one connected series on a single craft — prompt engineering — where each video assumes you watched the last. The execution problems from the First 10 are solved. What's left is a clean test of content and hook.

That test produced one unambiguous answer. Across nine published videos the channel stayed frozen at zero new followers — through a record save rate, record completion, even perfect search targeting. Then video 20 broke through with two. This is the post where I write down exactly what moved, what didn't, and the single lever that explains all of it. (V15 is held back unpublished, so nine of the ten carry real data.)

What Worked

The Two Breakouts

Two videos broke the pattern — and they broke it in completely different ways, which is the most useful thing in the whole dataset. V17 "Negative Constraints" ("tell ChatGPT what NOT to do") set the engagement records: 214 TikTok views, a 2.3% full-watch rate, a 5.07-second average watch, and a 4.27% Instagram save rate — every save-and-completion number peaked here. V20 "Emotion Prompting" ("tell it the stakes and it tries harder") set the depth-and-loyalty records: a 12.09-second average watch (2.4× V17's), a 2.6% completion rate, and the channel's first two followers.

The split is the lesson. V17 won on saves — a novel, contrarian tactic people wanted to keep. V20 won on follows — an emotional hook people felt. One is a bet on future usefulness; the other is a bet on you. The same channel, the same niche, two different peaks — and they map cleanly onto two different viewer actions. Hold onto that distinction; it's the key that unlocks the rest of the audit.

The Central Problem

Nine Videos. Zero Followers.

Here is the number that drove every decision in Vol 1: across nine straight published videos, new followers stayed at zero. Not low — zero. And it wasn't for lack of things going right. Each video won something, and not one of those wins converted into a follow:

  • V11 Role-Based — the benchmark. 189 TikTok / 121 IG / 24 YT, a clean baseline to measure against. 0 followers.
  • V12 Few-Shot — the YouTube high (112 views) and 5 TikTok saves at a 2.7% rate. 0 followers.
  • V13 Chain-of-Thought — strongest hook on paper, queued #1, earned the first comment of the series. 0 followers.
  • V14 Iterative Refinement — first full 3-platform read, and the first clear sighting of the 0:02 retention cliff. 0 followers.
  • V16 Instructional — the Instagram breakout: +355% to 123 views, a 2.44% save rate. 0 followers.
  • V17 Negative Constraints — every engagement record: 214 views, 2.3% full-watch, 4.27% IG save. 0 followers.
  • V18 Prompt Chaining — the workflow video. It flopped: 0 Instagram interactions, and a title ("break") that mis-targeted search. 0 followers.
  • V19 RAG — perfect search targeting ("chat gpt hallucinating") and a clean tactic, but the lowest watch time of the Vol (3.69s). 0 followers.

Records, breakouts, perfect targeting — and the follower count never moved off the floor. That isn't noise. That's a signal pointing at one specific thing.

"V19 fixed every variable the losses blamed — a tactic, perfect targeting, an early queue — and still died at 0:02. When you control for everything else and still lose, the hook is the only thing left."

The Experiment

How V19 Isolated The Hook

V19 "RAG" was the accidental controlled experiment of the series — the video that turned a hunch into a finding. Every earlier loss could be pinned on something: wrong content type, bad targeting, a late queue slot. V19 fixed all of them at once:

  • It was a tactic, not a workflow. "Paste your source" is one move — the same shape as V16's verbs and V17's do-nots, the content types that earned saves. V18's workflow had flopped; V19 controlled for that.
  • Its search targeting was perfect. The lone query that surfaced it was "chat gpt hallucinating" — the exact problem the video solves. The right viewers, arriving for the right reason.
  • It was queued early. Position #3 on TikTok, not buried mid-batch. Recency and queue position were both in its favor.
  • It hit the most relatable topic in the Vol. Everyone has caught ChatGPT making things up. Demand was not the issue.

Every variable optimized — and it still posted the lowest average watch time of the series (3.69s) and died at 0:02. When you control for content, targeting, queue, and demand and the result is still a loss, only one variable remains: the hook. V19's open — "AI just made up a fact, here's how to stop it" — is the single most generic problem-tease format in short form, and the scroll-trained brain filters it out before the second clause. That's the finding. The hook isn't a lever. It's the lever.

The Breakthrough

What Finally Bought The Follow

V20 "Emotion Prompting" changed exactly one thing and broke the streak. Instead of announcing a topic, its hook opened cold on a feeling: "this is very important to my career." The scroll-brain didn't pattern-match it to "another AI tip" — it read as a person with something at stake, and that bought the next second. Average watch time more than doubled to 12.09s, the 0:02 cliff finally had a deep engaged tail behind it, and two of the viewers who watched to the end became the channel's first followers.

That resolves the save/follow split from the top of this audit. A save is triggered by a novel tactic — useful, keepable, a bet on future need (V16's verbs, V17's do-nots). A follow is triggered by resonance — feeling something, and wanting more of the person who made you feel it. Nine videos chased saves and got them; none converted to follows, because usefulness and resonance are different currencies. V20 spent the second one. And the meta-joke writes itself: a video about telling the AI the stakes so it tries harder is the one whose hook made viewers care enough to follow. The technique it teaches is the technique that won it.

The Playbook

What Carries Into Vol 2

Vol 2 (videos 21–30) keeps the niche, the stack, and the connected-series format — and rebuilds the parts Vol 1 proved matter. Every rule below is earned from the data above, not borrowed from a guru.

  • The hook is the entire budget. V19 proved it by controlling for everything else and still losing. The first two seconds get more attention than the rest of the video combined, every time.
  • Open on a feeling, not a topic. V20's "this is important to my career" doubled watch time over V19's "AI just made up a fact." Never open with "X problem, here's the fix" — that template is invisible to the scroll-brain.
  • Design for depth, not reach. Follows come from finishers, not from views. V20 had near-baseline reach (192) but record depth (12.09s) — and that's what converted. Build for the people who stay.
  • Match the ask to the goal. Want saves? Teach a novel, contrarian tactic. Want follows? Make them feel something. They're different currencies — V17 earned one, V20 earned the other.
  • The niche is settled — stop second-guessing it. 35–54, male, US prompt-engineering practitioner. It held 9 of 9 videos. The follower problem was never who watched; it was whether the hook earned their depth. Build for this person and don't chase anyone else.
  • Tactics beat workflows. V18's multi-step workflow flopped (0 IG interactions) while single-move tactics (V16, V17) saved. Short-form wants one move you can screenshot, not a process.
  • Title for the search the viewer actually types. V18's "break" mis-targeted the algorithm; V19's exact-match phrasing nailed it. Lead the title with the words the viewer would search, not the clever ones.
  • YouTube is downstream of TikTok engagement. The 0% Shorts CTR is structural and not the lever — feed distribution tracks watch-depth signals. Win on TikTok depth and YouTube views follow (V20's 30 was the best since V12).
  • Keep the honesty. Every post is built from the real Whisper transcript, real screenshots, and a smell test — no cleaned-up numbers, no hindsight polish. That posture is the brand, and it stays.
// The One Thing To Remember

Vol 1 shipped ~2,670 views across nine clean, hand-built videos and learned one sentence the hard way: the niche is bedrock, the hook is the lever, depth is the currency, and emotional resonance is what finally buys the follow. Nine videos proved it by losing with everything else optimized; the tenth proved it by winning. Two followers isn't a milestone — it's two people — but it's the first evidence any of this works, and it came from being more human, not more clever.
