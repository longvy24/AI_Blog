Generic AI vs Your AI

An AI That Knows Your Exact Job

Generic AI is a brilliant intern who's never met you. You re-explain your format, your voice, your rules every single time — and it still gives you something almost right. A custom AI fixes that: you teach it your one specific job once, and from then on it just does it, your way, every time.

Here's mine, with the honest backstory attached. I started a faceless Reddit Story Time channel — the kind that reads viral Reddit posts over gameplay. It flopped. But to feed it, I'd built a small tool: a custom AI that scrapes Reddit's top stories and turns the best one into a ready-to-record script. The channel died. The tool didn't — it's still the best thing I own. Let me show you how it works, then how to build your own.

// The Job I Taught It (the system prompt, in plain English)
You turn Reddit's top stories into short-video scripts. For each post I give you, output my exact format — a hook, the key beats, a tone, and the literal closing line. No preamble. Same shape, every time.

That's it — that paragraph is the custom AI. I described the job to Claude in plain English, wrapped a tiny app around it to pull the Reddit posts, and now one click turns a messy thread into something I could record today. Claude wrote every line of the code, too.

Do It Yourself

Teach an AI Your Job

You don't need Reddit. You need the one repetitive, you-shaped task you keep re-explaining to a chatbot. Turn it into a custom AI:

  • Name the one job. The thing you do over and over with the same shape. "Turn a call transcript into a follow-up email in my voice." "Score a sales lead against my rules."
  • Write the job as instructions. In Claude, create a Project (or a saved system prompt) and describe the role, the rules, and the exact output format. Paste in a few of your best examples as the standard.
  • Feed it the real thing and correct it once. Run a real input, fix what's off, fold the fix back into the instructions. Two rounds and it's you.
  • Wrap a button around it if you want. Mine pulls Reddit and hits the AI on one click — Claude wrote that glue code too. Optional, but it turns a chat into a tool.
Why It Works

Context Is the Whole Game

A general model is capable of almost anything, which is exactly why it's mediocre at your specific thing by default — it's guessing your context every time. A custom AI front-loads the context permanently. The format, the voice, the rules, the examples — baked in once, so every run starts from "knows the job" instead of "meeting you for the first time."

That's why my flopped-channel tool still earns its keep: the channel needed an audience I couldn't manufacture, but the tool only needed my job description — and that never expired. Teach the AI once; it works forever.

The Honest Part

What a Custom AI Is and Isn't

No hype — and I'm the guy whose channel flopped, so trust me on the limits.

✓ Genuinely yours
  • An AI locked to your format and voice, no re-explaining.
  • Built from one plain-English job description, zero code.
  • Consistent output you'd actually ship — every run.
  • Improves by editing instructions, not retraining.
! Worth knowing
  • It's a sharp tool, not a strategy — it won't build the audience.
  • Garbage in still bites; it needs good source material.
  • You're the taste & the final check — it drafts, you decide.
  • API-wrapped tools cost pennies per run; budget for it.

The honest promise: a custom AI makes your repetitive, you-shaped work nearly free — but it's a tool, not a guarantee. My channel proved the tool can outlive the dream. That's not a sad ending; it means the thing you build to chase one idea quietly becomes useful for the next ten.

// The One Thing To Remember

Next time you catch yourself re-explaining the same task to a chatbot for the tenth time — stop. That's a custom AI waiting to be built. Describe the job to Claude once, lock in your format, and never explain it again. Even if the project it was for falls apart, the AI you taught is yours to keep.
