// THE TOOL I NEEDED
The Problem

Every Video Runs the Same Pipeline

There's a fixed path behind every episode of this series. A brief comes in. The hook gets confirmed. A script gets written, a voiceover gets generated, the thing fans out into three different cuts — one for YouTube, one for Instagram, one for TikTok — then merges back for a lint pass, a render, thumbnails, and a blog post like this one.

Nineteen steps. I run them in order, every time. And the entire thing lived in my head and in a text file. I couldn't look at it. I couldn't see where it branched, where things merged, or — the part that actually bugged me — which steps were quietly costing me money.

"To make this series, I follow one AI pipeline. I couldn't see it — so I built a tool that could."

The Ask

One Screen, and What It Costs

I wanted two things, and only two. First: the whole workflow on one screen, as boxes I could drag and wires I could redraw — so changing my process meant moving a node, not rewriting a document. Second, and more important: I wanted to know what each step actually costs.

That second one is the part no flowchart tool does. Every diagram app on earth will draw you boxes and arrows. None of them know that the "write the blog" step is an AI call that burns fourteen cents. I didn't want a picture of my pipeline. I wanted a priced picture of my pipeline.

// Roughly what I asked for
Build me a visual editor for AI workflows — a single self-contained page. A dark canvas with draggable nodes and wires I can connect. Each node can carry a model and a token estimate. Add up the total and show me the cost per run, live, as I edit. No account, no server — it all runs locally.
The Build

I Described It. It Shipped It.

I described that to Claude, and it built the app. Not a mockup, not a starting point — the working thing: the canvas, the drag handling, the connection logic, the pricing engine, the cost readout. I wrote zero lines of it. I still can't write a line of code, which is the entire premise of this series and still the part I find hardest to believe.

What I did do was the part that's still mine: notice things. The loop-back arrows rendered as straight lines cutting across the diagram, so I said so, and they got rerouted underneath. The editor panel was buried in cost-estimation fields that a normal person would never touch, so those got folded behind a collapsed "advanced" section. That's the actual job now — describe it, look at it honestly, say what's wrong, repeat.

What It Does

Drag a Node, Watch the Price

Every step is a node you can drag. Drop one on top of a wire and it splices itself into the flow; pull it off its lane and the chain heals behind it. Wire a node to many and you've got a branch; wire many into one and you've got a merge. That's the whole editing model, and it took about four minutes to learn because there isn't much to it.

The part I actually built it for is the number in the corner. Give a node a model and a rough token count, and the app totals it into a live cost per run. My production pipeline comes out at $0.185 a run — about 16k tokens across two billed steps — which at one video a day is $5.55 a month. Set a budget cap and the readout turns red when a change blows through it.

  • Fourteen worked examples built in — RAG, agent loops, multi-agent, guardrails, cost cascades. Load one and take it apart.
  • Compare two versions side by side — swap an expensive model for a cheaper one and see the monthly difference before you commit.
  • Bring your own workflow — import a JSON file, or describe your pipeline to any AI chat and paste the result straight in.
  • Export a PNG — the diagram at the top of this series' production notes came straight out of it.
The Honest Part

What It Is, and What It Isn't

This series only works if I'm straight about the limits, so: this is a planning tool, not a billing dashboard. It draws and prices your workflow. It does not run it.

✓ What's real
  • A genuine desktop app — Mac, Windows, Linux
  • Runs fully offline. No account, no telemetry
  • Current Claude list prices, and you can edit them
  • Free and open source on GitHub
~ What to know
  • It's an estimate — your real bill depends on real tokens
  • Calibrate it by importing your actual usage CSV
  • It doesn't execute the workflow, only maps it
  • Unsigned build — your OS warns on first open

The estimate caveat is the one that matters. Token counts are guesses until you measure them, so treat the number as a decision-making tool — "is this design roughly ten cents or roughly ten dollars?" — not as an invoice. That question is usually the only one you need answered before you build.

Where It Lives

It's Real, and It's Free

It's on GitHub as AI Workflow Studio — download the installer for your platform, or just open the single HTML file in a browser and it works the same. There's no signup, nothing to buy, and nothing phoning home.

I didn't build it to launch a product. I built it because I had a specific annoyance and the cost of fixing it had dropped to one conversation. That's the shape of most of what you'll make with AI — not a startup, just the tool your own week actually needs.

// The One Thing To Remember

The best first thing to build with AI is the thing that annoys you — because you already know exactly what it should do, and you're the only reviewer who matters. Don't look for a product idea. Look for the spreadsheet, the checklist, or the process living in your head, and describe it out loud.
