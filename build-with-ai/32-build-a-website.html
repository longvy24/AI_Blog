Why This Isn't a Template

"Build a Website" Used to Mean a Weekend

Until very recently, getting a real website meant one of three things: wrestle a drag-and-drop builder for a weekend, pay a developer four figures, or buy a template and spend hours bending it to fit. All three start at the same miserable place — the blank page — and most people with something worth putting online never get past it.

Last night I built a full landing page for a coaching business by giving Claude one sentence. No Squarespace, no template, no code. Hero, services, copy, a booking call-to-action — it wrote the whole page, section by section, while I watched. I want to be straight about where the line is, because the gap between the hype and the reality is exactly where the useful part lives.

// The Entire Prompt
Build a landing page for my coaching business — hero, services, and a booking call-to-action.

That's the whole brief. I gave it my business in one line, hit enter, and Claude started building the page — headline, layout, copy, buttons — one section at a time. I wasn't designing a thing.

"I'm not a designer. I wrote a sentence. Claude wrote the page — the layout, the copy, the buttons — and handed me a real site to scroll."

Do It Yourself

Build Yours Tonight

You don't need a coaching site. You need the move — and the whole thing runs on free Claude, where Artifacts (the live, runnable previews) work without paying.

  • Open Claude and start a new chat. No builder account, no template marketplace, no editor.
  • Give it your business in one line. Name the thing and what the page is for: "a landing page for my bakery with the menu and hours," "a one-pager for my freelance writing with samples and a contact form."
  • Let it build. Claude writes the page as an Artifact and renders it live, section by section. You watch — you don't touch the code unless you want to.
  • Tweak in plain English. "Make the hero bolder." "Add a testimonials section." "Use navy and gold." You're redesigning by talking. Repeat until it's yours.
Why It Works

Describe the Outcome, Not the Code

Same idea that powered episode one, and it's the whole thesis of this series: the model writes the code; you write the spec. "Hero, services, booking CTA" isn't a technical instruction — it's a description of what you want the page to do. The translation into HTML and CSS, the part that used to require a designer or a year of practice, is the part AI now handles.

So you stop aiming at the markup and start aiming at the result. The skill that matters isn't web design anymore — it's being able to say, clearly, what your page is for and who it's for. Get that right and the page builds itself.
