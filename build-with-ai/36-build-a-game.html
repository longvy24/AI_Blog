The Excuse That Died

"I Can't Code" Used to Mean "I Can't"

Making a game was the ultimate "someday" — the thing you'd do if you ever learned to program. For me that someday never came, because I can't write code. Not "I'm rusty" — I mean I couldn't build a working button on my own. A whole game with combat, loot, and bosses? Not a chance.

So I described one to Claude instead. The result is Grimoire: it's Text Twist — that anagram word game — crossed with a roguelike dungeon crawler. The twist that makes it mine: your words are the weapons. Every monster hands you a scrambled rack of letters, and the words you spell are how you attack. Claude wrote every line. Let me show you the build.

// The Pitch I Gave Claude
Make me a word game — Text Twist crossed with a roguelike. Each monster gives a scrambled 6-letter rack; spell words to attack, and longer/rarer words hit harder. Loot gear and spellbooks, descend through floors, fight bosses, permadeath.

That was the seed. From there Claude built the whole thing — and I shaped it by playing and giving notes. The word engine, the combat math, the loot tables, the bosses, the juice. I wrote zero lines of code. I was the designer; Claude was the entire engineering team.

"I can't write code. I still made a roguelike where your vocabulary is your damage. That sentence shouldn't be possible — and it is."

What Claude Built

A Real Game, System by System

This isn't a toy demo — it's a full roguelike loop. Here's the stack that came out of a description:

★ 01 · Word Engine
Text Twist, 29k words
A real word-game lexicon validates anything you spell; every rack is pre-verified to hold a full 3/4/5/6-letter spread, so battles are always solvable.
★ 02 · Word-Combat
Longer = harder hit
Letters have values, length multiplies damage (×1 → ×4.2), combos and crits stack. Spell the 6-letter word — the TWIST — and it devastates.
★ 03 · Loot & Spells
Gear that warps words
Weapons, armor, trinkets, spellbooks — with rarities and effects like "+dmg per vowel," "rare letters ×3," lifesteal. A merchant and a jackpot chest between floors.
★ 04 · Bosses & Depth
Descend · permadeath
Floors scale, bosses get traits (Armored / Regenerating / Frenzied), a battle clock enrages foes, and death is permanent. A run you can actually lose.

Do It Yourself

Make the Game In Your Head

You have a game idea. Everyone does — the one you'd make if you could. You can. Here's the loop:

  • Pitch the one-liner + the twist. Genre + the thing that makes it yours. "Snake, but the tail is your to-do list." "A typing game where fast words cast spells."
  • Ask for a playable version first. Have Claude build the core loop as a single runnable file before any polish. Play it in your browser in minutes.
  • Design by playing. "Too easy — make bosses hit harder." "Add a shop between levels." "The combo should decay faster." You feel the fun; Claude tunes the numbers.
  • Layer the juice last. Once it's fun, ask for the screen shake, the sound, the win screen. That's the difference between a prototype and something people replay.
Why It Works

The Bottleneck Was Never the Idea

Games are just rules plus feedback, and describing rules is something you already do fluently — it's the implementing that stopped you. That's the exact part AI is best at. You hold the vision and the taste; the model does the thousands of lines of logic, math, and state that used to be a wall between "I wish this existed" and "I'm playing it."

So the skill that matters isn't syntax anymore — it's knowing what's fun and being able to say it clearly. You don't need to code to make a game. You need an idea and the willingness to describe it.

The Honest Part

What AI Builds and What You Do

No hype — building a real game this way is genuinely doable, but here's the honest split.

✓ AI handles
  • All the code — engine, combat math, state, the juice.
  • Turning a description into a playable build in minutes.
  • Balance tweaks and new systems, on request.
  • Zero engine to learn, zero cost, runs in a browser.
! Still on you
  • Is it fun? Only you can feel that — AI can't playtest joy.
  • Scope: a big game is many rounds; ambition creeps.
  • You direct, cut, and decide — it drafts, you design.
  • Real "ship it" polish (portals, saves) takes intent.

The honest promise: AI removes the coding wall completely — but it doesn't remove the taste. Grimoire is fun because I kept saying "no, harder / faster / meaner" until it clicked. The game design is still human. The impossible part — building it at all — is the part that got free.

The One I Actually Built

A Whole Game, From Someone Who Can't Code

Grimoire is real. It's a complete run — floors, bosses, loot, a merchant, a jackpot chest, a local leaderboard — and it's built to ship (it wires in the CrazyGames SDK so it can go live on a game portal). Every line written by Claude, every design call made by me. The tools that run this channel — a trend analyzer, a Reddit script-writer, a thumbnail generator — are the useful stuff. Grimoire is the proof that "useful" isn't the ceiling: you can build the fun stuff too.

That's the part I want you to take: the barrier to making the thing you've always wanted to make just fell over. The whole story of the stack — the wins, the flops, why I started — is on the Start Here page. If a guy who can't code can build a roguelike, your "someday" game is a lot closer than you think.
