The Honest Cut

What I Actually Still Use

Here's the part the title promises. Building ten things is easy to romanticize — so let me be straight about which ones earned a place in my week and which were proof-of-concept. Both are wins; they're just different kinds.

✓ In my week
  • Get Ripped — I open it every training day.
  • Trend Analyzer — I run topics through it before filming.
  • Gist — highlight-to-summarize, constantly.
  • The landing page — it's the channel's live front door.
~ Proof, not habit
  • Invoice Maker, PeakForm — clean demos, not my daily need.
  • Sidebar — real, but a business is more than a build.
  • Grimoire — a joy to make; I play more than I "use" it.
  • The Stack — brand new; ask me in a month.

That's the honest read: about half of what I built became something I reach for; the rest proved a point. And that ratio is the good news — when building is nearly free, you can afford to make ten things to find the four that stick. You couldn't do that when each one cost weeks.

Why It Worked

The Bottleneck Was Never the Idea

Ten builds, ten domains, and the same pattern every time: I knew what I wanted, I could describe it, and the wall was always the implementation — the code, the layout, the plumbing. That's the exact part that got automated. Across an app, a game, a workout planner, and an extension, the human job never changed: have the idea, describe it clearly, and judge whether the result is any good.

That's the whole thesis of this season, proven ten different ways. You don't need to code to build real things anymore. You need taste, a clear description, and the nerve to hit send.
