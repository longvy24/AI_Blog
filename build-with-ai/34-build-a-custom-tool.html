Why You Settle for Almost

Stop Forcing Your Life Into Someone Else's App

You know the feeling: you download an app, and it does 80% of what you want — but the 20% it gets wrong is exactly the part you cared about. Too many features you'll never touch. A workflow that isn't yours. A subscription for the privilege. So you adapt to the tool, instead of the tool adapting to you. We've all just accepted that.

I couldn't find a tool that told me what to make next. So I stopped looking — and built my own. No code. One sentence to Claude, and a few seconds later I had a trend analyzer that does exactly what I want and nothing else. Let me show you the build, then the honest part about custom tools.

Do It Yourself

Build the One You Want

Forget trend analyzers — the move works for any small tool you wish existed. A tip splitter that divides by who actually ordered what. A workout logger with your exact lifts. A reading tracker, a meeting-cost calculator, a content scheduler. Here's the loop:

  • Open Claude and describe the tool in one line — exactly how you work, not how some app thinks you should. "A weekly meal planner with my five go-to dinners."
  • Ask for it as something you can use right now. Claude builds it as a live Artifact — click it, type in it, watch it work in seconds.
  • Refine by talking. "Add a fourth platform." "Show the top hashtags." "Bigger sentiment badges." You're editing a real app with sentences, not code.
  • Keep it — it's yours. No subscription, no bloat, no feature you'll never open. The tool fits you, instead of the other way around.
Why It Works

AI Collapses the Gap to "It Exists"

For your whole life, "I wish a tool did X" and "a tool that does X" were separated by a wall: you'd need to code it, or hope someone else built and sold it. So you settled.
