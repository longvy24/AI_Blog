How It Started

A Friend, and A Sport I Don't Watch

This one didn't start with a product idea. It started with a friend who bets on everything, and who talked me into it. I want to be clear about the setup, because it's the whole reason the tool exists: I am not a sports person. I couldn't tell you who's having a good season. I had money riding on games I wasn't watching.

Which meant I had no instinct to fall back on. My friend has one — or thinks he does. His entire model, as far as I can tell, is trust me bro. Mine was nothing at all. And the honest question that bugged me wasn't "how do I win." It was much smaller and much more answerable: when a pick came in, was that a good read or was I just lucky?

"My friend got me into sports betting. I'm not even into sports."

The Problem

Two Tabs, No Answer

The actual daily friction was mundane, which is usually where the good tools come from. I had a stats site open in one tab — ERA, park factors, platoon splits, all the real numbers. And I had my own picks in another: a slip of legs with no analysis attached to any of them.

Neither tab knew about the other. The stats page didn't know what I'd picked. My picks didn't know what the numbers said. So I was doing the comparison in my head, badly, across a browser window, for six games at once — and then forgetting the whole thing by the next day. There was no record. No way to look back and say: the model liked this one and it landed, or I liked this one and got bailed out.

// Roughly what I asked for
Build me a desktop dashboard that shows every game today on one board, live. For each one, work out a confidence score from the matchup — starting pitcher, park factor, platoon splits. Then keep a log of what the model expected versus what actually happened, so I can see where it was right and where it wasn't.
The Build

One Screen That Holds Both

I described it. Claude wrote it. What came back is a real desktop application — Electron, so it runs on Mac, Windows and Linux — not a script I babysit in a terminal. It's called C.R.E.A.M, and it does the one thing I actually wanted: it puts the games and the read on the same screen.

The board shows every game for the day. Finished ones collapse down and flag the winner. Live ones show a win-probability bar and where the game actually is. Tabs across the top for the other sports, because the same logic works whether it's baseball or football. I didn't write any of it.

The Model

Sabermetrics, and A Second Brain

The projections don't come from vibes. They come from sabermetrics — the same public, boring, well-established baseball statistics that analysts have used for decades. The model reads the matchup: who's starting, what the park does to run scoring, how a batter's numbers shift against that handedness.

But the part I'd actually defend as interesting isn't the projection. It's the memory. Every call the model makes gets logged, and then the real result gets logged next to it. Expected versus actual. Which means over time it isn't just making predictions — it's accumulating a record of how good its own predictions were.

  • Every game, live — score, state, and a win-probability bar as it moves
  • A confidence score per game, derived from the matchup rather than the odds
  • Matchup inputs you can name — starter, park factor, platoon splits
  • Expected vs actual, logged — so the model grades its own calls against reality
  • Multi-sport tabs, because the structure generalises past baseball

"A second brain logs what I expected against what actually happened."

The Honest Part

What It Is, and What It Isn't

This is the section that matters most on this particular post, so I'll be blunt. The dashboard does not tell me what to bet, and it doesn't place anything. It reads. It shows me the math and the record. Every decision after that is mine, and every consequence with it.

I'd also rather say the uncomfortable thing than let a nice-looking screen imply otherwise: a model that grades itself can still be wrong. A confidence score is a number, not a promise. A short run of results proves nothing in either direction — that's the entire reason the expected-vs-actual log exists, and it's also the reason I'm not going to quote any results from it.

✓ What's Real
  • A genuine desktop app — Mac, Windows, Linux
  • Read-only. It places no bets and no trades.
  • Inputs you can point at: pitcher, park, platoon
  • It keeps a record and grades itself against it
~ What To Know
  • A confidence model, not a winning system
  • Self-grading doesn't make it right — just accountable
  • Small samples prove nothing, in either direction
  • It will never tell you what to bet. By design.
Where It Lives

Built For One Person

Unlike most of this series, there's no link at the end of this one. C.R.E.A.M is a private build — a tool I made for myself, connected to my own accounts, and I'm not shipping an installer for something that sits this close to real money.

That's fine, and it's kind of the point. The interesting claim of this whole series was never "here's an app you should download." It's that the distance between an annoyance and a working tool is now one conversation. I had a specific, personal, faintly stupid problem — two browser tabs that wouldn't talk to each other — and a week later it was a desktop application with a model in it.

// The One Thing To Remember

Build the boring tool for the problem you actually have. Not the impressive one. Mine wasn't "predict sports" — it was "I can't tell if I was right." The second one is smaller, answerable, and turned out to be the useful thing to build.

// The Honest Version

I didn't build this because I love sports, and I won't pretend otherwise. The betting was my friend's idea. The modelling was the fun part. Park factors and platoon splits are a genuinely good engineering problem, and I'd probably have enjoyed building this for a sport I'd never wager a cent on.
