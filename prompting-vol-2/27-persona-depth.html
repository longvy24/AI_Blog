Why "Act as an Expert" Is Weak

A Job Title Is Not a Biography

Video 1 of the original series taught role prompting — assign ChatGPT a job title and watch the output get sharper. "Act as a marketing expert." "Act as a startup advisor." That's the floor. It works. But it's not the ceiling, and most people stop there.

The problem with a job title alone is that ChatGPT fills in the rest with averages. It writes like the median person with that title — competent enough to sound credible, generic enough to be useless. You get an answer that could have come from any consultant on any LinkedIn post about any topic. The role anchors the seat, but everything downstream — the actual expertise, the way the person thinks, what they refuse to do — is still default.

"A job title is the seat. A biography is the person sitting in it. ChatGPT gives you what you describe."

The 5 Layers

Build a Real Character

Every strong AI persona has five layers. Each one answers a specific question about the person, and each one constrains the output in a specific way. Skip any of them and the output drifts back toward "helpful assistant." Add all five and the output sounds like one specific human you'd hire.

★ 01 · ROLE — WHO
Who are they? What seat do they sit in?
Sets the chair. Without it, ChatGPT defaults to "helpful assistant" mode — generic, hedged, mid. Examples: senior brand strategist · tier-1 VC partner · principal designer.
★ 02 · CONTEXT — WHERE
What's the company, the stakes, the environment?
Constrains the advice. Strategy for a Series A startup is the opposite of strategy for a public company. Examples: Series A B2B SaaS · pre-PMF, 12-person team · B2C mobile.
★ 03 · EXPERIENCE — PROOF
What's the track record? Make it specific and quantified.
Earns the swagger. Vague experience produces hedged confidence. Quantified experience produces opinions. Examples: 3 launches past $10M · 80+ closed deals · 50+ apps shipped.
★ 04 · WORKING STYLE — HOW
How do they think? What's their default reflex?
Controls the tone. A skeptical-first persona pressure-tests your idea before agreeing. A people-pleaser one just validates. Examples: direct, no-fluff · skeptical first · task-not-aesthetic.
★ 05 · CONSTRAINTS — NEVER
What do they never do? Hard rules & pet peeves.
Locks in the discipline. Constraints stop the model from drifting back to default behavior. The "never" list is what makes the persona stick across a long chat. Examples: always quote a number · no hedge words · no "amazing."
// The 5-Layer Template
You are a [ROLE: senior X / principal Y / partner at Z]. You work at [CONTEXT: company stage + market + stakes]. You've [EXPERIENCE: quantified track record]. Your working style is [STYLE: how they think + default reflex]. You never [CONSTRAINTS: hard rules + pet peeves]. Now: [your prompt]
Three Personas To Steal

Built & Tested. Paste Them.

Three full personas I keep saved as snippets. Each one is built across all five layers — you can paste them verbatim or remix the slots. Use the right one for the kind of work you're doing.

★ THE INVESTOR · USE FOR STRATEGY DECISIONS
Tier-1 VC partner · 12 yrs · 80+ deals
"Looks for unit economics before the slide deck. Has passed on 8 unicorns and is fine about it. Believes the best founders argue back."
"You are a tier-1 VC partner, 12 years in the seat, 80+ deals closed. You focus on pre-Series A SaaS and consumer. You're skeptical first, specific second — you ask 'what does churn look like at month 6?' not 'how's retention?' You never softens bad news and every paragraph quotes a number."
★ THE EDITOR · USE FOR COPY + EDITING
NYT-bylined feature writer · 15 yrs
"Lives by 'lede before lecture.' Believes the second sentence is where bad writing dies. Has killed pieces at 4,000 words because the angle wasn't there."
"You are a feature writer at the New York Times, 15 years across politics + tech beats. You edit long-form features and Sunday cover stories. You cut every hedged word and flag passive voice in red. You always lead with the strongest line — never bury it past graf 2."
★ THE UX CRITIC · USE FOR DESIGN REVIEWS
Principal designer · 50+ apps shipped
"Critiques in user-tasks, not aesthetics. Once moved a button 4px and lifted activation 12%. Believes design debt is just deferred empathy."
"You are a principal product designer. 50+ apps shipped, 2 IPOs. You work on B2C mobile and B2B onboarding. You critique by user-task, not aesthetics — you open every review with 'what is the user trying to do right now?' You always end with one zero-cost fix the team can ship this sprint."
Why It Works

The Model Pattern-Matches On Detail

Under the hood, ChatGPT is doing pattern matching against everything it's seen. When you say "marketing expert," it pulls from the average marketing-content training data — which is, on average, mid. When you say "brand strategist at a Series A B2B SaaS who's launched 3 products past $10M in year one," the model has a much narrower band to pattern-match against. The output gets sharper because the specific persona has a specific voice the model can imitate.

The more real-sounding the persona, the better. Don't write "senior expert with lots of experience." Write "senior strategist, 12 years in the seat, 80+ deals closed." Quantify. Anchor. Make it sound like an actual person on an actual LinkedIn — because that's the pattern the model can lock onto.

Build Your Own

Five-Layer Checklist

  • Role: name the seat with adjective + specialty (not "engineer" — "senior backend engineer specializing in distributed systems")
  • Context: name the company stage, the market, and the stakes (not "tech company" — "Series A AI infra startup with 18 months of runway")
  • Experience: quantify it (years, count, names — "12 yrs, 80+ deals, Sequoia/a16z lineage" beats "lots of experience")
  • Style: describe the default reflex with an example ("skeptical first — asks 'what does churn look like at month 6?'")
  • Constraints: write the "never" list (not "be concise" — "never use the words 'great,' 'amazing,' or 'compelling'")
Common Mistake

Don't Write a Wishlist

The most common failure mode is treating the persona like a feature request. People stack adjectives — "expert, professional, knowledgeable, helpful, creative, analytical, concise, friendly…" and the model averages them out into nothing. Adjectives don't build a person. Specifics do. Replace every adjective with either a noun ("brand strategist") or a behavior ("asks for the number first").

The other failure mode is forgetting the constraints layer. Without "never" rules, the persona starts strong but drifts back to ChatGPT-default behavior after 3 or 4 turns in the chat. The "never" list is what keeps the persona locked in.

// The One Thing To Remember

Stop describing a role. Describe a person. Five layers — Role, Context, Experience, Style, Constraints — turn ChatGPT from a polite assistant into someone with opinions, taste, and a track record. Build one persona today, save it, and paste it every time you need expert output. It's the single biggest upgrade you can make to your default prompt.
