Why You Keep Re-Typing the Same Prompt

You're Already Using Templates

Look at your last 50 ChatGPT messages and you'll see the same prompts in slightly different shapes. "Write a cold email to a SaaS founder about onboarding." Three days later: "Write a cold email to a designer about portfolio reviews." Same prompt. Different inputs. You typed all the connective tissue twice — and you'll type it again next week.

The cost of typing-from-scratch isn't the seconds — it's the cognitive load. Every time you rebuild the same prompt, you make small judgment calls about structure, tone, and what to ask for. Those calls drift. Sometimes you forget to ask for a CTA. Sometimes the length blows out. The output quality varies because the input varies, even though you're trying to do the same thing.

A template fixes the structure once and lets you swap only the inputs. It's the difference between writing a contract from a blank page and filling in a contract template. The legal language doesn't change. The variables do.

"Prompts aren't paragraphs. They're mad libs. Build the structure once, fill the blanks forever."

The Anatomy of a Template

Four Brackets. That's the Whole Trick.

Every reusable prompt has the same four variables. Different jobs use different combinations, but if you anchor on these four you can template almost anything you write to ChatGPT. Each bracket is a slot you fill with a real value in 2 seconds.

★ 01 · TOPIC — WHAT
What's the subject?
The thing you're writing about. The more specific, the better the output. Examples: launch demo · Q3 plan · onboarding cuts · pricing change.
★ 02 · TONE — HOW
How should it sound?
The voice the model adopts. Different tones produce wildly different first sentences. Examples: direct · warm · witty · skeptical · contrarian.
★ 03 · AUDIENCE — FOR WHOM
Who is going to read this?
Shapes the vocabulary and the assumptions. An email to SaaS founders is the opposite of an email to your mom. Examples: SaaS founders · senior PMs · enterprise buyers · my team.
★ 04 · FORMAT — IN WHAT SHAPE
What's the output container?
Locks the structure. Same content reads differently as an email vs a tweet vs a memo. Examples: email · tweet · LinkedIn post · 1-page memo · agenda.
// The Universal Template
Write a [FORMAT] about [TOPIC] in a [TONE] tone for [AUDIENCE].

That's it. The four-bracket version covers maybe 60% of what most people prompt every week. Fill in the brackets and you have a complete instruction. Save this somewhere — Notes, a Raycast snippet, a TextExpander shortcut, a saved prompt in ChatGPT itself — and you'll stop typing the connective tissue forever.

Four Templates To Steal

Built, Tested, Paste Them.

Four templates I keep saved as shortcuts. Each one is built for a specific job — outreach, social, research, meetings — and each one bakes in the structure so you don't have to think about it. Fill the brackets and ship in under 15 seconds.

★ 01 · COLD EMAIL · USE FOR OUTREACH
The Cold Email Mold
When: cold outbound, warm follow-ups, intro asks, partnership pings — anywhere a generic "hope this finds you well" email won't cut it.
"Write a cold email to a [persona] decision-maker about [topic]. Structure: one-sentence hook using [insight] → 2-sentence value prop → single clear CTA. Direct, no fluff. Max 90 words."
"Hey [Name] — saw you just closed Series A. Most teams lose ~40% of activations to onboarding friction. We fix it in 14 days, fixed price. 15-min look?"
★ 02 · SOCIAL CAPTION · USE FOR LINKEDIN/X/IG
The Social Hook Mold
When: you're posting to LinkedIn, X, IG, or TikTok and need an opener that doesn't sound like every other "thrilled to announce" post on the timeline.
"Write a [platform] caption about [topic]. Hook style: [hook type] (question / bold claim / stat). 2-3 punchy sentences. End w/ [CTA]. Match native platform tone. Hashtags."
"73% of SaaS companies undercharge by 2-3x. We tested 50 pricing pages last quarter. The ones that won? They all did one thing different. ↓ #SaaS #Pricing"
★ 03 · RESEARCH SUMMARY · USE FOR LONG SOURCES
The Research Brief Mold
When: you've got a 40-page report, an hour-long transcript, or a dense PDF, and you need it filtered for your specific angle in under 5 minutes.
"Summarize this source: [source]. Deliver exactly 3 numbered takeaways written from my angle: [my angle]. Each with a direct quote under 20w. End with one open question."
"1. Self-serve wins on speed: 'Activation in <10 min beats sales-led every time.' 2. Power users drive 80% of revenue. 3. Pricing pages are the highest-leverage page. Open Q: Does this hold for $50K+ ACV?"
★ 04 · MEETING AGENDA · USE FOR 1:1S, SYNCS, RETROS
The Meeting Agenda Mold
When: a meeting goes on the calendar and you don't want it to drift — kills the "what are we even doing here" moment in the first 30 seconds.
"Create an agenda for a [meeting type] with [attendees]. Desired outcome: [outcome]. Max 5 items: title · owner · time block · decision or artifact produced. Flag out-of-scope."
"1. Recap H1 misses (CEO · 10min · decision: kill or pivot) 2. Top 3 H2 bets (CPO · 20min · artifact: 1-pager) 3. Resourcing (CFO · 10min · decision) · Out-of-scope: hiring."
Why It Works

Specificity in the Brackets Beats Adjectives

Templates work because they force you to be specific where it matters and skip where it doesn't. "Write a punchy, engaging, professional email" is three adjectives the model averages out into nothing. "Write a cold email to a Series A CTO about onboarding cuts, max 90 words" gives the model four specific things to lock onto. The brackets are where the work happens. The connective tissue is where the laziness should happen.

The other thing templates buy you: consistent quality across drafts. When you type from scratch, your prompt drifts based on your mood, the time of day, how much coffee you've had. A template removes the variance. You ship the same structural-quality prompt every time, which means the model ships the same structural-quality output every time. Output reliability comes from input reliability.

Build Your Own

Four-Bracket Checklist

  • Pick a job you do weekly — outreach, social, summaries, agendas, code reviews, anything you re-type more than 3x
  • Write the prompt once, in full, the way you'd say it on your best day — direct, specific, with structure
  • Wrap the variables in brackets — anything that changes between uses becomes [bracket]. Anything that stays becomes the template's spine
  • Save it as a snippet — Raycast, TextExpander, Notes, or directly inside ChatGPT's saved prompts. The trigger should be 3 keystrokes or fewer
  • Use it 5 times this week — edit the bracket values, not the structure. After 5 real uses, refine the spine; never refine on theory
Common Mistake

Don't Over-Engineer the Spine

The most common failure mode is treating the template like a control panel — adding bracket #5 for "word count," bracket #6 for "reading level," bracket #7 for "must include statistic." Every extra bracket is a slot you have to fill, which means another decision every time you use the template, which means you stop using the template. The whole point is to remove decisions, not add them.

The other failure mode: writing the template once and never touching it again. Templates should evolve. After 10 real uses you'll notice the structure that drops the ball — maybe the CTA is always weak, maybe you keep editing the same line out. Update the spine. The template is a living thing, not a stone tablet. Refine on data, not vibes.

// The One Thing To Remember

Stop typing prompts from scratch. Build the mold once. Fill it forever. Four brackets — Topic, Tone, Audience, Format — cover most of what you write to ChatGPT every week. Steal the four templates above (Cold Email, Social Hook, Research Brief, Meeting Agenda), save them as snippets, and ship in 10 seconds instead of two minutes. Your prompts will get more consistent. Your output will get sharper. And you'll never type "Hey, hope this finds you well" again.
