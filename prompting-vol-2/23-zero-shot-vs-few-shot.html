Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot —
I Tested Both.

Two prompting methods, same task, wildly different outputs. I ran the head-to-head test. One clearly wins on creative work. The other holds its own on the simple stuff. Here's the verdict and how to know which to reach for.

The Two Methods

One Asks. The Other Teaches First.

The naming is technical, but the difference is dead simple. Zero-shot prompting means you just ask the model to do the thing. No examples, no setup. "Write a tweet announcing our launch." That's it. The model has to figure out what you mean and what good output looks like, all from one sentence.

Few-shot prompting means you paste in a couple of examples of the kind of output you want, then ask for the new one. "Here are two example launch tweets I love: [paste]. Now write one for our launch." The model gets to study the pattern before performing.

That's the entire technical difference. Examples or no examples. Everything that follows — when each one wins, when each one loses — comes down to what showing those examples actually does to the model's output.

"Zero-shot is a pop quiz. Few-shot is a pop quiz with the study guide attached."

The Head-to-Head

Same Task. Two Runs.

I ran the same two tasks through both methods in fresh ChatGPT chats, with no system prompt and no chat history. Test 1: write a tweet announcing a product launch — a craft-y, voice-driven creative task. Test 2: extract all the dates from a paragraph and return them as a JSON array — a structured, mechanical task where there's basically one correct shape for the answer.

Then I scored both outputs out of 10. Generic phrasing, hedge words, and corporate softening lost points. Tightness, voice, and format-fidelity won points. Here's how it shook out.

Why The Gap

Examples Override The Default.

On the creative task, the few-shot version absolutely cooked. The zero-shot output came back with the AI's default "announcement tweet" energy — emoji, "we're excited to share", a generic call to action. The few-shot version, after seeing two tight examples, dropped all of that and matched the tone perfectly. Same model, same task, completely different output.

On the structured task, both versions tied. Why? Because the task only has one good shape — a JSON array of date strings. The model's default behavior was already what I wanted. The examples didn't help because there was no taste or voice to teach. Few-shot only helps if you're correcting the model's defaults; if the defaults are already correct, you're just adding tokens.

// The Few-Shot Template
Here are 2 examples of [output type] in the style I want: Example 1: [paste a good one] Example 2: [paste another good one] Now, in that same style, [your actual task].
When To Use Each

The Decision Rule

  • Use few-shot when: the output has voice, tone, or format that the model's default doesn't nail (tweets, captions, brand copy, taglines, hooks, anything stylistic)
  • Use few-shot when: you need consistency across many similar outputs — give one set of examples, then run the same task with different inputs
  • Use zero-shot when: the task has one obvious "correct" output shape (data extraction, summarization, simple Q&A, structured JSON)
  • Use zero-shot when: you're exploring or brainstorming and you actually want the model's default takes, not a clone of your examples
  • Use zero-shot when: speed matters — fewer tokens in means faster responses
Common Mistake

Bad Examples Hurt More Than Help

The biggest mistake people make with few-shot is using mediocre examples. The model treats your examples as the gold standard. If you paste two okay tweets, you'll get an okay tweet back — and you've spent more tokens to get the same quality as zero-shot. Few-shot only pays off when your examples are visibly better than the model's default would be.

The other mistake: too many examples. Two is usually the sweet spot. Three can help. Five or more starts to bias the model in ways you didn't intend — it starts cloning small accidental patterns in your examples instead of the actual style you wanted. Keep your few-shot prompts few.

// The One Thing To Remember

Few-shot wins when you're shaping style. Zero-shot wins when you need speed. The skill isn't picking a favorite — it's reading the task in two seconds and reaching for the right method. If the output has voice, paste examples. If the output has one obvious shape, just ask.
